A 60-year-old woman comes to the office due to 10 days of diffuse itching. A rash began on her hands and quickly spread to involve her wrists, arms, and axillae. The patient has difficulty sleeping and complains of intense itching in these areas. She works in a day care center and primarily cares for children age 2-5. The patient has no other medical conditions and takes no medications. Vital signs are normal. BMI is 24 kg/m2. Skin examination shows small, erythematous papules on the palms with excoriations, scattered vesicles, and pustules on the finger webs, palms, and wrist creases, as seen in the image below. She has similar lesions in the axillary skin folds bilaterally. The remainder of the examination is within normal limits.

Which of the following is the most likely finding on skin sampling of this patient's lesions?

A. Budding yeast
B. Molluscum bodies
C. Multinucleated giant cells
D. Necrotic keratinocytes in the epidermis
E. Sarcoptes scabiei mite and mite eggs

    • Explanation

    This patient's rapidly spreading pruritic rash with erythematous papules and excoriations is suggestive of scabies, or infestation by the Sarcoptes scabiei mite. The mite spreads through direct person-to-person contact and burrows into the skin. A history of working in a setting with close interpersonal contact (eg, day care center) is common.

    The manifestations of scabies are due to a delayed type IV hypersensitivity reaction to the mite, mite feces, and mite eggs. Patients have an intensely pruritic rash that is often worse at night. The characteristic distribution includes the flexor surfaces of the wrist, lateral surfaces of the fingers, and finger webs; it also commonly involves the axillae and extensor elbow surfaces. Skin examination usually shows excoriations with small, crusted, red papules scattered around the affected areas; vesicles, pustules, or wheals may also be present. Linear burrows are the most specific finding but are often obscured by excoriations.

    Diagnosis is usually clinical but can be confirmed by skin scrapings showing mites, ova, and feces under light microscopy.

    (Choice A) Budding yeast is characteristic of Candida infection, which usually presents with erythematous plaques and erosions with satellite papules/pustules in areas with increased moisture and friction, such as skin folds (eg, axillae, genital areas). This patient's palmar involvement makes this diagnosis unlikely.

    (Choice B) Molluscum bodies are seen with molluscum contagiosum, which is caused by a DNA poxvirus. Lesions may be pruritic but appear as small, firm, pearly papules with central umbilication. Moreover, molluscum contagiosum does not involve the palms and soles.

    (Choice C) Epidermal multinucleated giant cells are seen with herpes simplex virus (HSV) and varicella-zoster virus (VZV) infections. In contrast to this case, HSV causes vesicular eruptions involving the perioral or genital regions, while VZV causes a vesicular rash that is diffuse (primary infection) or of limited dermatomal involvement (shingles).

    (Choice D) Necrotic keratinocytes in the epidermis suggest Stevens-Johnson syndrome, a severe reaction that occurs 1-3 weeks after an infection (eg, cytomegalovirus, Mycoplasma) or drug exposure. Unlike this case, patients usually develop high fever, mucosal erosions, and skin detachment.

    Educational objective:

    Scabies is a highly contagious disease that presents with an intensely pruritic, erythematous, papular rash (usually worse at night) involving the flexor surfaces of the wrist, sides and webs of the fingers, and axillae. Diagnosis can be confirmed by skin scrapings from excoriated lesions that show mites, ova, and feces under light microscopy.
