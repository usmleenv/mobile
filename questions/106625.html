A 60-year-old woman is enrolled in a longitudinal research study for individuals with a family history of Parkinson disease. The patient's mother died from complications of idiopathic Parkinson disease 2 years ago. The study consists of symptom questionnaires, serial neurologic examinations, and brain imaging. Review of systems is notable for chronic constipation, which is progressively worsening. The patient also recently began having difficulty double-clicking her mouse. Vital signs show orthostatic hypotension. Neurologic examination reveals normal muscle bulk, tone, and strength with no abnormal movements. Postural stability is maintained. Gait speed and stride length are normal. Advanced brain imaging reveals 50% loss of nigrostriatal volume. Compared to an age-matched healthy individual, which of the following is most likely present in this patient's remaining nigrostriatal neurons?

A. Blockade of postsynaptic dopamine receptors
B. Decreased tyrosine hydroxylase activity
C. Increased presynaptic dopamine reuptake
D. Increased synaptic dopamine concentration
E. Upregulation of postsynaptic dopamine receptors

    • Explanation

    This patient is participating in a prospective study of those at elevated risk (eg, positive family history) for Parkinson disease (PD). She has no cardinal motor symptoms of PD: bradykinesia, tremor, or rigidity. However, neuroimaging reveals significant nigrostriatal atrophy, with 50% loss of volume.

    It is critical to note that motor impairment is not evident until a major loss (typically >60%) of substantia nigra (SN) dopaminergic neurons has already occurred. Neuronal loss begins 10-20 years prior to the onset of typical motor parkinsonism. During this period, known as prodromal or premotor PD, powerful compensatory mechanisms counteract presynaptic dopamine deficiency:

    • In the surviving presynaptic SN neurons, de novo dopamine synthesis is stimulated by the upregulation of tyrosine hydroxylase expression and activity (Choice B).
    • In the synapse, dopamine reuptake is inhibited due to the downregulation of dopamine transporters. This assists in preserving dopamine levels in the synaptic cleft (Choice C). However, because there is severe loss of SN dopaminergic neurons overall, total synaptic dopamine levels are still lower than those of healthy subjects (Choice D).
    • In the postsynaptic striatal neurons, there is compensatory proliferation of dopamine receptors (particularly D2R) and enhanced receptor clustering, which work in tandem to augment dopamine signaling.

    As nigrostriatal dopamine depletion worsens, these compensatory mechanisms are defeated, and the motor symptoms of PD are unmasked. Very subtle motor difficulties, especially in tasks requiring finesse and nimble movement (eg, double-clicking mouse), may be the earliest findings.

    Because nonmotor manifestations are driven by alpha-synuclein neuronal toxicity, rather than dopamine deficiency, they are the hallmark of prodromal PD. Autonomic dysfunction is common due to alpha-synuclein invasion of the enteric nervous system (gut hypomotility, constipation) and vascular sympathetic nerves (orthostatic hypotension), as seen in this patient. Invasion of the brainstem sleep centers produces REM behavior disorder, a highly specific prodromal presentation; 90% of patients are diagnosed with an alpha-synucleinopathy within 10 years.

    (Choice A) Blockade of postsynaptic dopamine receptors in the basal ganglia occurs in drug-induced parkinsonism (eg, antipsychotic agents), which is clinically indistinguishable from idiopathic PD. In drug-induced parkinsonism, SN dopaminergic neurons are intact and synaptic dopamine levels are normal.

    Educational objective:

    The majority of substantia nigra dopaminergic neurons (>60%) are lost prior to the onset of motor symptoms in idiopathic Parkinson disease. During this prodromal period, dopamine depletion is offset by several compensatory mechanisms, including the upregulation of postsynaptic dopamine receptors.
