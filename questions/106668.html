The patient feels mildly feverish but has no other symptoms. Medical history is notable for multiple sclerosis and associated trigeminal neuralgia. Medications include a recombinant anti-B-cell antibody that was started a year ago and carbamazepine, which the patient has been on for the past 2 weeks. Temperature is 38 C (100.4 F), blood pressure is 118/86 mm Hg, and heart rate is 86/min. Examination shows a rash on the chest, abdomen, back, and proximal extremities, as shown in the exhibit.

    This patient developed a morbilliform (ie, measles-like) rash 1-2 weeks following carbamazepine initiation, concerning for morbilliform drug eruption, a type of drug-induced exanthem.

    Morbilliform drug eruption is a type IV (delayed-type) hypersensitivity reaction caused by drugs or their metabolites. It typically manifests 5-21 days following drug initiation, or within 1-3 days after reexposure in previously sensitized patients. Lesions consist of erythematous papules and macules distributed symmetrically on the trunk and extremities, sometimes accompanied by low-grade fever, pruritis, and eosinophilia. Unlike Stevens-Johnson syndrome, mucosal involvement (eg, conjunctivae, oropharynx) and skin tenderness are absent.

    Common culprit drugs include anticonvulsants (eg, carbamazepine, phenytoin), antibiotics (eg, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole), and antiretroviral therapy. Treatment involves cessation of the offending drug; the rash typically self-resolves within 1-3 weeks.

    (Choice B) Bacterial folliculitis presents as discrete erythematous follicular papules and pustules. Confluent macules and nonfollicular papules are not characteristic.

    (Choice C) In addition to fever, measles typically presents with conjunctivitis, cough, and coryza before the rash erupts; these symptoms are absent in this patient. In addition, measles is rare, particularly in adults.

    (Choice D) Scarlet fever is associated with streptococcal pharyngitis and presents with diffuse, fine, erythematous papules with a sandpaper-like texture. This patient does not have sore throat and lacks tonsilitis on examination.

    (Choice E) Caused by Staphylococcus aureus, toxic shock syndrome presents with high fever, hypotension (ie, shock), vomiting, diarrhea, altered mentation, and a diffuse erythematous macular rash. This patient has normal blood pressure and lacks symptoms of a serious systemic illness.

    Educational objective:

    Morbilliform drug eruption is a type IV hypersensitivity reaction caused by drugs (eg, anticonvulsants, antibiotics) or their metabolites that typically occurs 5-21 days following drug initiation. Lesions consist of erythematous macules and papules, distributed symmetrically on the trunk and extremities, sometimes accompanied by low-grade fever. Mucosal involvement is absent.
