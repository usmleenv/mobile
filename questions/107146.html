A 15-year-old boy is brought to the emergency department due to worsening rash and profuse sweating for the past week. Symptoms began gradually, and the patient first noticed the sweating on a cold day, which he thought was unusual. He has also felt generally unwell, with difficulty sleeping and sensitivity to light. Physical examination shows an anxious-appearing boy with obvious hyperhidrosis. Temperature is 36.7 C (98.1 F), blood pressure is 170/96 mm Hg, pulse is 112/min, and respirations are 16/min. Skin examination is significant for an erythematous maculopapular rash over the torso, thighs, and upper arms, as well as pink macules over the palms and soles with associated desquamation. Neurologic examination shows a slight tremor. Excessive exposure to which of the following is most likely responsible for this patient's presentation?

    This patient has signs of catecholamine excess (eg, diaphoresis, tachycardia, hypertension) in addition to neuropsychiatric symptoms (eg, insomnia, tremor) and a desquamating rash. This constellation of findings warrants evaluation for chronic mercury toxicity.

    Manifestations of mercury poisoning include the following:

    • Cardiovascular/sympathetic nervous system: Mercury inactivates catechol O-methyltransferase, an enzyme responsible for catecholamine breakdown. Therefore, mercury toxicity leads to increased serum and urinary epinephrine and norepinephrine, causing excess stimulation of the sympathetic nervous system (similar to pheochromocytoma). Symptoms include diaphoresis, tachycardia, hypertension, and anxiety.
    • Neuropsychiatric: Mercury can cross the blood-brain barrier and deposit in the CNS, specifically the cerebral cortex, leading to a range of psychiatric and motor symptoms (eg, personality changes, insomnia, tremor); photophobia may also be seen.
    • Dermatologic: Mercury toxicity, particularly in children, can lead to a hypersensitivity rash (acrodynia). A diffuse, pruritic body rash or a desquamating, pink rash on the palms and soles is characteristic.

    Exposure to mercury is usually occupational (eg, gold miner). However, a high degree of suspicion must be maintained in children because they are typically exposed inadvertently via broken household items (eg, thermometers, lightbulbs), which may be revealed with a detailed history.

    (Choice A) Inhalant (eg, hydrocarbons) abuse can manifest with CNS symptoms such as agitation, but more severe symptoms (eg, ataxia, disorientation, hallucinations) are also typical and present acutely (not gradually). In addition, the rash associated with inhalant abuse is typically perioral, unlike the truncal and palmar rashes seen in this patient.

    (Choice B) Tremor, psychiatric symptoms (eg, depression), and hypertension can be symptoms of chronic lead exposure. However, this patient's diaphoresis and desquamating dermatitis make this diagnosis unlikely.

    (Choice C) Marijuana toxicity can result in acute psychosis or chronically, cannabis hyperemesis syndrome or respiratory disease. Profound diaphoresis and desquamating rash are not expected.

    (Choice E) Selenium toxicity typically manifests as gastrointestinal disturbance (eg, nausea, diarrhea) and hair/nail loss; catecholaminergic manifestations (eg, hypertension, tachycardia, diaphoresis) are not expected.

    Educational objective:

    Mercury impairs the breakdown of catecholamines, and toxicity can lead to excessive stimulation of the sympathetic nervous system (eg, tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis). A desquamating hypersensitivity rash can also occur, and deposition in the CNS can result in personality changes, insomnia, and tremor.
