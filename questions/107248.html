A previously healthy 34-year-old woman comes to the emergency department due to 3 days of severe headache. The patient has had a global headache that has progressively worsened and has now become intolerable. She has also had nausea but no fever, emesis, or diarrhea. The patient works as an international reporter and, over the past month, has traveled extensively in Europe and Asia. CT scan of the head is normal. Cerebrospinal fluid results from lumbar puncture are as follows:

Opening pressure 280 mm H2O
Glucose 60 mg/dL
Protein 110 mg/dL
Leukocytes 1,000/mm3
Neutrophils 5%
Lymphocytes 10%
Eosinophils 85%
Red blood cells 10/mm3

Which of the following is the most likely cause of this patient's illness?

A. Angiostrongylus cantonensis
B. Cryptococcus neoformans
C. Mycobacterium tuberculosis
D. Herpes simplex virus
E. West Nile virus

    This patient has a severe headache, nausea, and an elevated eosinophil count in the cerebrospinal fluid, indicating eosinophilic meningitis, a condition most often caused by Angiostrongylus cantonensis infection. Humans are incidental hosts and typically acquire the helminth by eating contaminated mollusks or snails while in Southeast Asia or the Pacific Islands. Eosinophilic meningitis can also be caused by other helminths (eg, Gnathostoma, Baylisascaris) or, less commonly, by fungi (eg, coccidioidomycosis), leukoproliferative disorders (eg, Hodgkin lymphoma), or an adverse drug reaction.

    Eosinophils are of the granulocyte lineage (along with basophils and neutrophils), and the majority of total body eosinophils are in tissue (eg, spleen, lymph nodes, thymus, digestive tract). However, polyclonal expansion of peripheral eosinophils can occur due to release of IL-5 by TH2 lymphocytes and innate lymphoid cells in response to helminth infection, allergens, drugs, or rheumatic disease; IL-5 may also be constitutively expressed by solid organ malignancies.

    (Choice B) Cryptococcus neoformans can rarely cause eosinophilic meningitis. However, cryptococcal infections primarily occur in those with profound immunocompromise (eg, AIDS) and typically present with subacute manifestations (eg, fever and headache over 1-2 weeks). This patient who is otherwise healthy and recently traveled to Asia is far more likely to have Angiostrongylus cantonensis.

    (Choice C) Mycobacterium tuberculosis is possible in a patient who travels extensively to endemic areas. Although tubercular meningitis may occur, it causes lymphocyte-predominant meningitis; eosinophilic meningitis is not seen.

    (Choice D) Herpes simplex virus can cause encephalitis due to infection of the temporal lobe. Patients usually present with fever, focal neurologic deficit, and/or seizure. Dramatically elevated erythrocytes (due to cerebral microhemorrhage), not eosinophils, are typically seen on cerebrospinal fluid analysis. In addition, brain imaging generally reveals temporal lobe abnormalities.

    (Choice E) West Nile virus is usually marked by a lymphocyte-predominant meningitis; profound cerebrospinal fluid eosinophilia is uncommon.

    Educational objective:

    Eosinophilic meningitis is most often due to helminth infection (eg, Angiostrongylus). Peripheral eosinophilia is largely mediated by the release of IL-5 in response to helminths, allergens, drugs, or rheumatic disease.
