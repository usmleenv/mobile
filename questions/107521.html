A 16-year-old previously healthy girl is brought to the emergency department due to intermittent episodes of high fever, chills, and rigors for the past 2 days. She reports drenching sweats when the fever resolves but has no other symptoms. The patient recently returned from a 2-week trip to Kenya. Temperature is 39.4 C (102.9 F), blood pressure is 114/68 mm Hg, and pulse is 118/min. Physical examination shows a normal oropharynx, clear lungs, no heart murmurs, and a nontender abdomen. Microscopic examination of Giemsa-stained blood smears demonstrates the suspected pathogen. Which of the following events is most likely directly causing this patient's fever?

A. Circulation of sexual forms of the pathogen
B. Inoculation of the host by the vector
C. Pathogen invasion into hepatocytes
D. Release of a toxin from lysed pathogens
E. Rupture of pathogen-infected erythrocytes

    This patient's febrile illness and positive Giemsa-stained blood smear shortly after returning from Kenya suggest malaria. The infection is transmitted when an infected female Anopheles mosquito injects Plasmodium sporozoites during a bite. The sporozoites travel through the bloodstream to the liver, infect hepatocytes, and mature to schizonts over 1-4 weeks. This period is asymptomatic (Choices B and C).

    Once the hepatic schizonts are mature, they rupture the hepatocyte, releasing thousands of merozoites into the bloodstream. The merozoites rapidly infect circulating erythrocytes and mature over 24 hours from ring forms ‚Üí trophozoites ‚Üí mature schizonts. The lysis of infected erythrocytes by mature schizonts triggers the release of inflammatory cytokines, which cause the fever and other systemic symptoms of malaria.

    Initially, most patients have periods of high fever that occur at irregular intervals; over time, the intervals become more regular (every 24 - 48 hr) due to synchronized rupture of erythrocytes by schizonts. Most patients also have nonspecific symptoms such as diaphoresis, cough, headache, abdominal pain, and myalgia. Examination often demonstrates splenomegaly due to pooling of damaged and infected erythrocytes in the splenic sinusoids. Laboratory evaluation usually shows anemia and mild hyperbilirubinemia.

    (Choice A) A small minority of merozoites released from infected erythrocytes mature into gametocytes, which cause no symptoms. Gametocytes circulate in the bloodstream until they are taken up by a feeding female Anopheles mosquito, which begins the life cycle anew.

    (Choice D) Plasmodium species do not harbor a toxin that is released when they are destroyed. Gram-negative bacteria release endotoxin (eg, lipopolysaccharide) when they are lysed, which can cause septic shock.

    Educational objective:

    Malaria is generally asymptomatic for 1-4 weeks after transmission. Manifestations arise once the pathogen lyses erythrocytes, which releases inflammatory cytokines. Initial manifestations typically include periods of high fever and nonspecific, flu-like symptoms.
