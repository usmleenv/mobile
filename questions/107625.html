A 7-year-old boy is brought to the clinic for follow-up care. The patient recently completed a course of antibiotics for a urinary tract infection caused by Escherichia coli. He has had 2 febrile urinary tract infections in the past, both of which resolved following antibiotic therapy. Vital signs are normal. Examination of the abdomen is unremarkable. Renal ultrasound examination reveals no signs of hydronephrosis. Voiding cystourethrogram shows contrast filling the bladder and extending linearly toward the umbilicus. This patient most likely has a congenital abnormality related to which of the following structures?

A. Omphalomesenteric duct
B. Umbilical artery
C. Umbilical vein
D. Urachus
E. Ureter
F. Urorectal septum

    This patient's imaging findings are consistent with a congenital urachal anomaly. In utero, the caudal yolk sac forms a tubular outpouching called the allantois, which extends from the embryologic bladder to the umbilicus. As the bladder descends into the fetal pelvis, it stretches the allantois, which becomes thin and narrow. Normally, epithelial proliferation obliterates the lumen of the allantois by 12 weeks gestation, forming a fibrous cord known as the urachus.

    Failed or inadequate epithelial proliferation leads to a complete or partial patent urachus:

    • Complete patent urachus: Neonates usually have urinary umbilical drainage on presentation due to a continuous connection from the bladder to the umbilicus.
    • Partial patent urachus: A range of defects may occur depending on the location of the patency, including a urachus that is open only to the umbilicus (urachal sinus), to the bladder (urachal diverticulum), or centrally (urachal cyst).

    This patient with a normal abdominal examination most likely has a urachal diverticulum, as seen on imaging as a linear extension of urine from the bladder toward the umbilicus. Urinary stasis within the diverticulum is a risk factor for recurrent urinary tract infection, as seen in this case.

    (Choice A) Similar to the allantois, the omphalomesenteric (or vitelline) duct is a tubular outpouching of the yolk sac that involutes in early gestation. However, because it connects the yolk sac to the fetal midgut, failed obliteration results in a connection between umbilicus and bowel (not bladder).

    (Choices B and C) The umbilical arteries and vein are contained within the umbilical cord, and abnormalities (eg, single umbilical artery) are usually isolated. These vascular structures are not connected to the bladder lumen.

    (Choice E) The ureters are derived from the mesonephric duct, and abnormalities may result in ectopic ureteral insertion along the mesonephric system (eg, urethra). A midline bladder diverticulum directed toward the umbilicus would not be expected.

    (Choice F) The urorectal septum divides the cloaca into the distal anorectal and urogenital regions. Developmental abnormalities would not explain this patient's abnormal urinary flow from the bladder to the umbilicus.

    Educational objective:

    Congenital anomalies of the urachus result in an abnormal connection between the bladder and the umbilicus. Full patency results in urinary umbilical leakage, whereas partial patency can result in a range of defects, including a bladder diverticulum (increases the risk of urinary tract infection due to stasis of urine).
