A 14-year-old girl is brought to the clinic for evaluation of primary amenorrhea. The patient takes no medications. Examination establishes breast development consistent with Tanner stage 1 and pubic hair consistent with Tanner stage 3. There is a blind vaginal pouch. Imaging reveals absence of a uterus and fallopian tubes. Epididymides and vasa deferentia are present bilaterally, and testes are visualized bilaterally within the inguinal canal. Karyotype analysis shows 46,XY. Which of the following underlying mechanisms is most likely responsible for this patient's condition?

A. Absent conversion of testosterone to dihydrotestosterone
B. Aplasia of fetal Leydig cells
C. Complete defect in androgen receptor function
D. Inability to convert 17-hydroxyprogesterone to 11-deoxycortisol
E. Loss of the sex-determining region of the Y chromosome

    DHT = dihydrotestosterone.

    This patient has a 46,XY genotype, external female genitalia, and internal male genital ducts (vas deferens, epididymis) with testes. Discordance between anatomy and genotype is diagnostic of a disorder of sex development, and in this case the findings are consistent with 5-alpha reductase deficiency.

    Leydig cells within the fetal testes in genotypic males (XY) secrete testosterone, which induces wolffian duct differentiation into male internal genital ducts. Some testosterone is also converted to dihydrotestosterone (DHT) by the enzyme 5-alpha reductase. DHT induces genital tubercle differentiation into male external genitalia. In the absence of 5-alpha reductase and DHT, the genital tubercle differentiates into female external genitalia, as in this patient.

    Because the uterus, fallopian tubes, and proximal vagina derive from the M√ºller duct, which involutes in the presence of antim√ºllerian hormone (AMH) secreted by Sertoli cells in the fetal testes, these structures are absent, and the vagina ends in a blind pouch.

    Most patients have a female phenotype at birth, and the disorder is diagnosed at puberty due to the development of secondary male sexual characteristics (eg, body hair, increased muscle mass from rising testosterone) or primary amenorrhea. Breast development (ie, gynecomastia) does not occur because increased serum testosterone (relative to estradiol) inhibits breast tissue proliferation.

    (Choice B) Leydig cells secrete testosterone (required for development of internal male genital ducts). The presence of epididymis and vas deferens is inconsistent with Leydig cell aplasia.

    (Choice C) Genotypic males (XY) with complete androgen insensitivity syndrome are phenotypically female and have primary amenorrhea due to absent internal female genital ducts (from appropriate AMH action). In contrast to this case, however, internal male genital ducts (vas deferens, epididymis) would be absent and gynecomastia (not Tanner stage 1) would be expected due to the nonfunctional androgen receptors.

    (Choice D) In patients with the XX genotype, 21-hydroxylase deficiency (ie, inability to convert 17-hydroxyprogesterone to 11-deoxycortisol) causes virilization. A genotypic male (XY) with 21-hydroxylase deficiency would have normal male external genitalia.

    (Choice E) Genotypic males (XY) with loss of the sex-determining region of the Y chromosome (ie, SRY gene) do not develop testes. Absence of testicular hormones (testosterone, AMH) results in internal female genital ducts and external female genitalia.

    Educational objective:

    The enzyme 5-alpha reductase converts testosterone to dihydrotestosterone, which drives development of external male genitalia. Genotypic males (XY) with 5-alpha reductase deficiency develop female external genitalia but have internal male genital ducts (eg, vas deferens) instead of internal female genital ducts (eg, uterus).
