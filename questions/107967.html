A 40-year-old woman is evaluated for extreme weakness, headache, blurry vision, and difficulty swallowing and opening her eyes over the past several hours. Her husband has similar symptoms and is being evaluated in the emergency department as well. One day earlier, they both developed abdominal cramps and diarrhea after attending a lunch event at a cultural heritage center. Temperature is 36.9 C (98.4 F), pulse rate is 58/min, blood pressure is 100/68 mm Hg, and respirations are 18/min. The patient is alert, awake, and fully oriented. She has difficulty supporting her head and is visibly drooling. Upper extremities are weaker than the lower extremities. Sensation is intact throughout. Which of the following is the most likely source of this patient's infection?

A. Improperly preserved beans
B. Infection from a sexual partner
C. Undercooked freshwater fish
D. Undercooked shrimp
E. Water contaminated by human feces

    • Explanation

    This patient and her husband had gastrointestinal symptoms followed by rapidly progressive neurologic deficits that include:

    • double vision and ptosis
    • bulbar symptoms (eg, dysphagia, drooling)
    • symmetric, descending muscle weakness (arms > legs)
    • intact sensation
    • absence of systemic inflammatory symptoms (eg, afebrile, no tachycardia, normal mental status)

    This presentation is suspicious for foodborne botulism. Certain improperly canned foods, particularly nonacidic vegetables (eg, beans) and meat, provide the appropriate environment for Clostridium botulinum spore germination. As the bacteria grow, they produce a toxin that inhibits presynaptic acetylcholine release at the neuromuscular junction. This leads to rapidly progressive, symmetric, descending weakness (limb and/or bulbar). Because it is caused by release of a preformed toxin, foodborne botulism usually does not induce an inflammatory reaction, which is why patients are typically afebrile and not tachycardic.

    (Choice B) Although some sexually transmitted infections can lead to neurologic symptoms (eg, neurosyphilis), the presentation would be less acute.

    (Choice C) Diphyllobothrium latum can contaminate freshwater fish and lead to gastrointestinal symptoms (eg, diarrhea, intestinal obstruction) and pernicious anemia; the resultant neurologic symptoms due to B12 deficiency would take years to manifest. Consumption of pufferfish can result in acute neurotoxicity from tetrodotoxin, which blocks the influx of sodium into nerve axons, but pufferfish are primarily saltwater dwelling.

    (Choice D) Undercooked shrimp can lead to infections such as Vibrio vulnificus, which causes severe diarrhea. Patients also frequently develop sepsis and evidence of disseminated intravascular coagulation.

    (Choice E) Common infections due to feces-contaminated water include Escherichia coli and hepatitis. Although neurologic symptoms may occur, they are not the typical presenting features. In addition, a shared community meal is unlikely to expose participants to feces-contaminated water.

    Educational objective:

    Foodborne botulism is caused by the ingestion of food contaminated with botulinum toxin, often from improperly home-canned foods. It leads to eye and bulbar symptoms with symmetric descending muscle weakness; fever and mental status changes are typically absent.
