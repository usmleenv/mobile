A 1-day-old girl is evaluated in the newborn nursery due to swollen hands and feet. She was born at term via cesarean delivery. The newborn's vital signs are normal. Examination is significant for a short, webbed neck; dysplastic nails; and bilateral, nonpitting carpal and pedal edema. Ultrasound examination of the abdomen reveals a horseshoe kidney. Which of the following is the most likely cause of this infant's edema?

A. Excessive urinary sodium retention
B. Impaired left ventricle contractility
C. Increased capillary permeability
D. Increased urinary loss of protein
E. Lymphatic network dysgenesis

    This newborn has edema involving the hands and feet. Edema develops due to accumulation of fluid in the interstitial space. It can be categorized as pitting (ie, makes a physical indentation with applied pressure) or nonpitting.

    • Pitting edema typically results when there is increased fluid extravasation into the interstitial space. Common causes include increased capillary hydrostatic pressure (eg, right-sided heart failure, venous thrombosis) and decreased intravascular oncotic pressure (eg, nephrotic-range proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia in cirrhosis) (Choice D).
    • Nonpitting edema occurs when there is impaired drainage of excess interstitial water due to lymphatic obstruction (eg, malignancy, congenital hypoplasia) or accumulation of water-binding mucopolysaccharides in the interstitial space (ie, pretibial myxedema in hypothyroidism). Increased capillary permeability (eg, angioedema) also characteristically causes nonpitting due to rapid extravasation of protein-rich fluid that overwhelms the normal drainage pathways (Choice C).

    In this case, the patient has nonpitting edema in addition to a webbed neck, nail dysplasia, and horseshoe kidney, a constellation of findings that is characteristic of Turner syndrome (TS). Swelling occurs in more than half of patients with TS and is due to lymphatic network dysgenesis causing congenital lymphedema. Poor lymph absorption leads to the accumulation of fluid in the interstitial space of the hands and feet. Impaired drainage of cervical lymph results in formation of a webbed neck (ie, short neck with loose skin), and severe obstruction of lymphatic vessels can result in cystic hygroma and hydrops fetalis.

    (Choice A) Sodium (and therefore fluid) retention occurs with kidney failure and causes increased capillary hydrostatic pressure and edema that is pitting, in contrast to this patient's nonpitting edema. In addition, although patients with TS are predisposed to horseshoe kidney, renal function is usually normal.

    (Choice B) Impaired left ventricle contractility initially causes left-sided heart failure and presents with signs of pulmonary edema, such as increased work of breathing (eg, tachypnea), hypoxia, and crackles (findings not seen in this patient). Over time, increased pulmonary pressure can cause increased central venous pressure and lead to right-sided heart failure, but edema would be pitting, unlike the edema in this patient.

    Educational objective:

    Nonpitting edema often develops due to lymphatic outflow obstruction (eg, malignancy, congenital malformation). Congenital lymphedema due to lymphatic network dysgenesis is common in Turner syndrome, which is also characteristically associated with webbed neck, horseshoe kidney, and nail dysplasia.
