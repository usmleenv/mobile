A newborn girl is being evaluated in the nursery. She was born at term via spontaneous vaginal delivery. Examination shows fusion of the third and fourth fingers bilaterally with an extra digit present between these 2 fingers. The remainder of the examination is unremarkable. Laboratory work-up confirms a mutation in HOXD13, a gene involved in regulating craniocaudal body patterning during embryonic development. This gene encodes a protein that directly regulates body patterning by which of the following mechanisms?

A. Alternatively splicing pre-mRNA
B. Binding DNA transcription regulatory regions
C. Modulating DNA methylation patterns
D. Modulating histone acetylation
E. Selectively initiating mRNA translation

    This patient has a mutation in a gene that regulates craniocaudal body patterning in utero, or a homeobox (HOX) gene. HOX genes are highly conserved DNA sequences that regulate positioning of embryonic structures. There is a spatial relationship between the location of the homeobox gene and its expression along the craniocaudal axis: genes located near the 3‚Ä≤ end gene cluster express cranially, and genes located near the 5‚Ä≤ end gene cluster express caudally.

    HOX genes code for transcription factors that bind regulatory regions on DNA of target genes involved in the segmental organization of the embryo. By either activating or silencing target gene expression, these transcription factors perform a key function in directing cell differentiation and positioning in early embryogenesis.

    Mutations in a HOX gene lead to malpositioned body parts. This patient has webbing between digits (syndactyly) and a supernumerary finger in between (polydactyly) due to a mutation in HOXD13, a homeobox gene that regulates limb development.

    (Choice A) Alternative splicing of pre-mRNA may occur during aberrant homeobox gene expression, contributing to congenital malformation. However, homeobox genes themselves do not regulate body patterning by alternative splicing.

    (Choices C and D) Modulation of DNA methylation or histone acetylation patterns (ie, epigenetic modification) can result in altered gene expression without any change in the DNA sequence. Epigenetic changes can be inherited by daughter cells, and are responsible for normal cellular differentiation and X-chromosome inactivation. Activation of oncogenes via histone acetylation and/or inactivation of tumor suppressor genes by DNA methylation also occur during the development of nearly all cancers. Although homeobox genes influence epigenetic modification, they function directly as transcription factors.

    (Choice E) Regulation of protein synthesis at the translational level can occur via phosphorylation of various initiation, elongation, and release factors. Homeobox genes regulate body patterning by targeting transcription, not translation.

    Educational objective:

    Homeobox genes code for transcription factors that bind regulatory regions on target genes involved in the segmental organization of the embryo along the craniocaudal body axis. Mutations in homeobox genes lead to congenital malformations involving positioning and patterning (eg, synpolydactyly in HOXD13 mutation).
