A 52-year-old man with hypertension begins taking a new antihypertensive agent that is expected to enhance natriuresis while also causing a sustained decrease in serum angiotensin II concentration and aldosterone production. The effects of this medication are most similar to which of the following drug classes?

A. Alpha-1 adrenergic blocker
B. Angiotensin II receptor blocker
C. Direct renin inhibitor
D. Loop diuretic
E. Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist

    • Explanation

    Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) overactivity can have a significant role in the development of hypertension. Renin is produced in the juxtaglomerular cells of the kidney in response to hypoperfusion. Renin cleaves angiotensinogen into angiotensin I, which is converted to angiotensin II by angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) in the lungs. Angiotensin II is a potent vasoconstrictor that also promotes aldosterone production in the adrenal cortex. Aldosterone acts on the collecting ducts to increase renal sodium and water reabsorption, which increases intravascular blood volume and blood pressure.

    Any medication that inhibits RAAS activity will enhance natriuresis via a reduction in aldosterone activity. The only listed medication that enhances natriuresis while also decreasing angiotensin II level and aldosterone production is a direct renin inhibitor (eg, aliskiren). ACE inhibitors (eg, lisinopril) prevent conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II and also enhance natriuresis while decreasing angiotensin II levels and aldosterone production.

    (Choice A) Alpha-1 adrenergic blockers (eg, terazosin, doxazosin) are vasodilators that are occasionally used to treat hypertension. These drugs decrease blood pressure via direct vasodilation and do not decrease angiotensin II or aldosterone concentrations or induce natriuresis.

    (Choice B) Angiotensin II receptor blockers (eg, losartan) prevent angiotensin II from acting on angiotensin receptors. These drugs reduce aldosterone production and induce natriuresis, but they increase angiotensin II levels (the RAAS is blocked after angiotensin I is converted to angiotensin II).

    (Choice D) Loop diuretics (eg, furosemide) induce natriuresis but do not inhibit RAAS activity. The decreased blood volume caused by loop diuretics actually stimulates renin release, which then increases angiotensin II and aldosterone concentrations.

    (Choice E) Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (eg, spironolactone) block aldosterone receptors to induce natriuresis. However, angiotensin II and aldosterone levels are increased.

    Educational objective:

    The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) is integrally involved in the pathophysiology and treatment of hypertension. RAAS activity can be reduced by several classes of antihypertensives, all of which will induce natriuresis by decreasing the effects of aldosterone. Direct renin inhibitors and ACE inhibitors accomplish this by lowering circulating levels of angiotensin II.
