A newborn boy is being evaluated in the nursery. The patient was born at term via spontaneous vaginal delivery. A cystic lesion is noted overlying the lumbosacral spine region. The remainder of the physical examination is unremarkable. Ultrasound imaging confirms a neural tube defect. The mother had no prenatal care, and further discussion reveals that she took a medication known to be teratogenic during pregnancy. Exposure to the medication most likely occurred during which of the following weeks of embryonic development?

A. The week prior to fertilization
B. Week 1
C. Week 4
D. Week 16
E. Week 32

    • Explanation

    This patient has a neural tube defect. Like other major congenital anomalies, neural tube defects develop during the embryonic stage of fetal development, or 3-8 weeks after fertilization (ie, weeks 5-10 of gestation). During this crucial time, the embryo is undergoing organogenesis, or rapid cell division and differentiation of all major organ systems, making it particularly susceptible to teratogens.

    The beginning of the embryonic stage is marked by gastrulation, which establishes 3 distinct cell layers: ectoderm, mesoderm, and endoderm. All tissues and organs are derived from these 3 layers. Structures formed by the end of the embryonic stage include the gastrointestinal tract and lungs (endoderm); the heart, kidneys, and spleen (mesoderm); and the skin and central nervous system (ectoderm).

    The central nervous system develops via neurulation during the third and fourth weeks. During neurulation, the neural plate forms from the ectoderm, and the lateral edges of the plate (neural folds) migrate toward the midline and fuse. Fusion begins in the cervical region and extends rostrally and caudally, forming the neural tube (precursor to the brain and spinal cord). The ends, or neuropores, close at the end of the fourth week. In this case, exposure to a teratogenic medication (eg, folic acid antagonist such as valproic acid) likely occurred during neurulation, interfering with caudal neural tube closure and causing a neural tube defect.

    (Choices A and B) Fertilization is the process by which the male and female gametes form a zygote. The zygote transforms into a blastocyst, which implants in the uterine wall approximately 1 week after fertilization. Significant teratogenic exposure prior to gastrulation usually results in spontaneous abortion or no abnormalities, as the undamaged pluripotent cells can fully compensate if enough survive.

    (Choices D and E) The central nervous system matures throughout pregnancy and can be affected by teratogens beyond the first 8 weeks (eg, alcohol causing fetal alcohol syndrome). However, defects related to neural tube formation (neurulation) occur only during the early embryonic period.

    Educational objective:

    Organogenesis occurs in the embryonic stage of development (3-8 weeks after fertilization), during which time the embryo is particularly susceptible to teratogens. Neurulation (neural tube formation) occurs during weeks 3 and 4, and abnormalities in this process result in neural tube defects.
