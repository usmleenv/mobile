A 56-year-old man comes to the emergency department due to fever and malaise for the past day. The patient was recently diagnosed with diffuse large B-cell lymphoma and completed the first cycle of combination chemotherapy 10 days ago. Temperature is 38.4 C (101.1 F). Physical examination is unremarkable with the exception of diffuse lymphadenopathy. The patient is hospitalized, and empiric antibiotics are initiated. Two days later, blood cultures drawn on admission grow yeast forms with pseudohyphae and demonstrate an ability to form germ tubes. Which of the following immunodeficiency mechanisms is most likely responsible for the invasiveness of this patient's infection?

A. Complement deficiency
B. Hypogammaglobulinemia
C. Low eosinophil count
D. Low neutrophil count
E. Low T-lymphocyte count

    • Explanation

    This patient underwent cytotoxic chemotherapy and subsequently developed a bloodstream infection with Candida albicans, a small, elliptical, budding yeast. Visualization of pseudohyphae, a chain of budding yeasts joined together with end-to-end at constrictions, is an important diagnostic clue. C albicans is distinguished from other Candida species by its ability to form germ tubes (true hyphae with no constrictions) when incubated at 37 C (98.6 F) for 2-3 hours (ie, germ tube test).

    C albicans is a human commensal organism that may cause mucocutaneous infection in otherwise healthy individuals but rarely causes invasive disease. Protection against infection is mediated by the following:

    • Intact epithelium: Candida triggers toll-like receptors on epithelial cells to release proinflammatory cytokines, which recruit immune cells to the area; epithelial cells also release beta-defensins, which provide antifungal activity.
    • Resident macrophages/monocytes: Penetration of epithelial cells by fungal hyphae causes resident macrophages to release inflammatory cytokines that recruit lymphocytes, monocytes, and neutrophils to the area of infection. T helper lymphocytes specific for Candida antigens help drive the epithelial and macrophage response, and are important for prevention of superficial Candida infection (eg, oral/esophageal, cutaneous, vulvovaginitis).
    • Neutrophils: Neutrophils are the most important immune cell in the protection against invasive Candida (eg, candidemia, deep tissue infections). Neutrophils kill Candida by releasing reactive oxygen species, lactoferrin, lysozyme, elastase, and beta-defensins. They are also the only immune cell that is able to prevent germination of Candida yeasts into penetrating hyphae.

    Patients who undergo cytotoxic chemotherapy and develop neutropenia are at dramatically increased risk for invasive infection. Indwelling central intravascular catheters are also a major risk factor for invasive disease.

    (Choice A) Early complement proteins (eg, C3b, C5b) assist opsonization, and late complement proteins (C5-C9) form the membrane attack complex. Complement is crucial for control of bacterial infections, but it plays a minimal role in the control of Candida. In addition, complement levels are not particularly diminished by chemotherapy.

    (Choice B) Candida is primarily controlled by neutrophils, macrophages, and helper T cells; immunoglobulins have more of an adjunctive role. In addition, immunoglobulin levels are not typically affected by most cytotoxic chemotherapy because memory B cells are long lasting and have low rates of cellular division.

    (Choice C) Although chemotherapy impairs the production of granulocytes, which include eosinophils, basophils, and neutrophils, transient reduction in eosinophil and basophil count does not drastically increase risk of infection.

    (Choice E) T lymphocytes (particularly Th1 and Th17 subtypes) primarily prevent superficial Candida infection by strengthening the mucosal immune defense; conditions that impair cell-mediated immunity (eg, HIV) are associated with higher risk of mucocutaneous Candida infection (eg, thrush, esophagitis). However, protection against invasive disease is mediated primarily by neutrophils, not lymphocytes.

    Educational objective:

    Neutrophils are the most important immune cell in the defense against invasive Candida infection; therefore, patients with neutropenia (eg, following cytotoxic chemotherapy) are at high risk for invasive disease (eg, candidemia, meningitis). In contrast, T lymphocytes are more important for prevention of superficial, mucocutaneous infection (eg, thrush).
