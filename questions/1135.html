A 4-year-old boy is brought to the clinic by his mother due to decreased appetite, abdominal cramps, and diarrhea over the last 3 days. The stools were initially watery but have now become bloody. Temperature is 38 C (100.4 F). Mucous membranes are dry, and the abdomen is mildly distended and diffusely tender to palpation. Stool studies are positive for fecal leukocytes and occult blood.     • Explanation

    This patient's fever, abdominal cramps, and bloody diarrhea (dysentery) are consistent with bacterial enteritis. Stool culture results are most specific for Shigella species, which are non‚Äìlactose fermenting organisms with no hydrogen sulfide production on triple sugar iron agar (unlike Salmonella). In addition, Shigella ferments glucose, in contrast to Campylobacter, a curved rod that does not.

    Shigella is transmitted via the fecal-oral route (eg, person-to-person, contaminated food/water). Once in the gastrointestinal tract, the bacteria is able to invade the intestinal epithelium via the microfold (M) cells that overlie Peyer patches. After cell entry, Shigella lyses its containment vacuole, multiplies within the cytoplasm, and exits the basolateral surface of the cell where it can more easily enter enterocytes and then spread to adjacent cells via host-cell actin polymerization.

    Shigella also induces macrophage apoptosis, triggering a robust host inflammatory response. The disrupted epithelial barrier leads to ulcerations and leakage of blood, inflammatory cells, and mucus into the intestinal lumen, resulting in bloody, mucoid stools.

    (Choice A) Shiga toxin is an exotoxin that damages intestinal mucosa by inactivating the 60S ribosomal subunit, halting cellular protein synthesis and causing hemorrhagic colitis. However, not all pathogenic Shigella species produce Shiga toxin; Shigella sonnei and Shigella flexneri (prevalent in resource-rich regions) do not produce Shiga toxin but still cause shigellosis, making mucosal invasion (common to all Shigella species) the primary pathogenic mechanism.

    (Choice B) Asymptomatic colonization with Shigella does not occur because the bacterium is highly pathogenic. Ingestion of as few as 10 organisms can lead to mucosal invasion and active infection.

    (Choice D) Salmonella and Yersinia enterocolitica, which also invade M cells, can gain access to the lymphatics and proliferate in the mesenteric lymph nodes. In contrast, once Shigella invades the M cells overlying lymphoid follicles (Peyer patches), it spreads primarily to adjacent enterocytes; it does not proliferate in lymphoid tissue.

    (Choice E) Shigella rarely causes bacteremia because it is readily phagocytosed and destroyed by neutrophils after entering the bloodstream. Enteric bacteremia is more likely due to E coli or Salmonella typhi (typhoid fever).

    Educational objective:

    Shigella is a non‚Äìlactose fermenting organism that does not produce hydrogen sulfide on triple sugar iron agar. Mucosal invasion is the primary pathogenic mechanism for Shigella infection, which presents with bloody/mucoid diarrhea.
