A 10-year-old boy is brought to the clinic due to nausea, vomiting, generalized headache, ataxia, and vision disturbances. The headaches have been occurring daily for the past 6 weeks and wake him from sleep. MRI reveals a tumor in the cerebellum. Biopsy of the tumor shows a well-differentiated neoplasm composed of cells that have hair-like glial processes along with eosinophilic corkscrew-shaped fibers and granular bodies. Based on these findings, which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Ependymoma
B. Glioblastoma
C. Medulloblastoma
D. Neuroblastoma
E. Pilocytic astrocytoma

    • Explanation

    Biopsy of this patient's cerebellar mass reveals features consistent with pilocytic astrocytoma (PCA). PCAs are the most common pediatric brain tumor and typically arise infratentorially, specifically within the cerebellum, often leading to headaches, nausea, vomiting, and cerebellar dysfunction (eg, ataxia). Less commonly, PCAs may arise in the brainstem, hypothalamus, or optic pathway.

    Microscopic examination frequently reveals a well-circumscribed tumor containing bipolar astrocytes with ovoid nuclei and hair-like (ie, piloid) processes. These processes contain glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP), indicative of glial lineage. Proteinaceous, eosinophilic, cytoplasmic inclusions with a corkscrew appearance (ie, Rosenthal fibers) and eosinophilic granular bodies (thought to be derived from lysosomes) are also often present.

    (Choice A) Ependymomas often arise near the ventricular system and may also cause headaches, nausea, vomiting, and ataxia. However, histopathologic examination typically shows ependymal cells arranged around a central blood vessel (ie, perivascular pseudorosette).

    (Choice B) Glioblastomas are malignant glial tumors that occur most often in adults. They are often located in the cerebral hemispheres and may cross the corpus callosum. They are composed of atypical (eg, pleomorphic, hyperchromatic) astrocytes that often accumulate around regions of necrosis (ie, pseudopalisading). Microvascular proliferation is also commonly present.

    (Choice C) Medulloblastomas, another common pediatric brain tumor, also primarily arise within the cerebellum. However, histopathologic examination often shows small, round, blue tumor cells that may be arranged around a central core of neuropil (ie, fibrillary material containing neuronal processes), forming Homer-Wright rosettes.

    (Choice D) Neuroblastomas, the third most common childhood cancer after leukemia and CNS tumors, are embryonal neuroendocrine tumors typically found in the adrenal medulla or sympathetic ganglia. Histopathologic examination often shows small, round, blue tumor cells that can form Homer-Wright rosettes.

    Educational objective:

    Pilocytic astrocytoma is the most common pediatric brain tumor and is typically found in the cerebellum. Histopathologic findings often include a well-differentiated neoplasm composed of cells with hair-like processes that stain positively for glial fibrillary acidic protein. Eosinophilic corkscrew-shaped (Rosenthal) fibers and eosinophilic granular bodies are frequently present.
