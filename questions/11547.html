A 24-year-old man comes to the emergency department due to swelling of his left leg after his dog bit him yesterday. The dog is fully vaccinated and has lived with him for the past year. The bite caused a break in the skin and bleeding. The patient irrigated the wound with bottled water and soap and managed his pain with acetaminophen. Today, the area around the wound is warm, red, and swollen. Temperature is 36.4 C (97.5 F). Physical examination shows an open wound on the left leg with surrounding erythema, warmth, and tenderness. No wound drainage or crepitation is present. The distal pulses are palpable. Wound cultures grow gram-negative coccobacilli; the culture has a mouse-like odor.     This patient with localized erythema, warmth, and tenderness has a skin and soft tissue infection at the site of a recent dog bite. Pathogens in animal bite wound infections include animal oral flora and/or human skin flora, and infections are often polymicrobial. One of the most frequently identified pathogens after a cat or dog bite is Pasteurella multocida; Pasteurella canis is also common in dogs.

    Pasteurella species are part of the normal upper respiratory tract flora in many healthy wild and domestic animals regardless of vaccine status. The bacteria are gram-negative coccobacilli with a mouse-like odor (due to indole positivity), as noted in this case. Other common pathogens in dog bite infections include human skin flora (eg, Staphylococcus, Streptococcus), anaerobes, and Capnocytophaga canimorsus.

    Infection typically develops within 24 hours after inoculation and often presents as cellulitis (eg, erythema, warmth, tenderness). Management includes wound care and antibiotics (eg, amoxicillin-clavulanate).

    (Choice A) Infection with Bartonella henselae, a gram-negative bacterium causing catscratch disease, typically causes a pustule/nodule with regional lymphadenopathy several days after inoculation. However, B henselae is transmitted via a scratch or bite from an infected cat (transmission from dogs is not common).

    (Choice B) Campylobacter jejuni is usually transmitted via contaminated food and causes a diarrheal illness, not cellulitis.

    (Choice C) Skin infection with Clostridium perfringens (found in soil) usually occurs after penetrating trauma (eg, stab wound) and leads to myonecrosis and gas gangrene.

    (Choice D) Exposure to animals infected with Coxiella burnetii (eg, cattle, sheep) can cause Q fever resulting in a mild flu-like illness or pneumonia.

    (Choice E) Erysipelothrix rhusiopathiae is commonly found in domestic and marine animals and can cause a localized cutaneous infection (erysipeloid) typically following occupational exposure (eg, fishery, slaughterhouse). However, it is a gram-positive rod (not a gram-negative coccobacillus).

    (Choice F) Francisella tularensis is a gram-negative bacterium that causes tularemia, a zoonotic infection that usually presents with regional lymphadenopathy and an ulcerative lesion at the inoculation site. This infection typically occurs following contact with animals such as rabbits and rodents, not dogs.

    (Choice G) Fusobacterium is an anaerobe that is part of normal oral flora and can be seen in soft tissue infections after a dog bite. However, infection with Pasteurella is more common, particularly in this patient whose cultures have a mouse-like odor.

    (Choice I) Proteus mirabilis is a common cause of urinary tract infections, not cellulitis.

    Educational objective:

    Soft tissue infections following cat and dog bites are typically due to animal oral flora, the most common of which is Pasteurella.
