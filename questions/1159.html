A 65-year-old postmenopausal woman comes to the office for an annual visit. The patient feels well and has no symptoms. She has no chronic medical conditions, and her only medication is a daily multivitamin. Physical examination shows no abnormalities. Screening DXA scan reveals mild osteopenia. The patient is advised regarding appropriate lifestyle measures to limit bone loss, including weight-bearing exercise. In response to increased mechanical load, cells located within bone lacunae (indicated by the arrow) communicate with each other via gap junctions to increase bone remodeling within regions experiencing excessive strain.

The cells described above are most likely to represent which of the following?

A. Mesenchymal stem cells
B. Osteoblasts
C. Osteoclasts
D. Osteocytes
E. Osteochondroprogenitor cells
F. Pericytes

    • Explanation

    This postmenopausal woman with low bone mineral density is at increased risk for osteoporosis and bone fracture. Moderate weight-bearing exercise helps prevent further bone loss by altering osteocyte activity through a process known as force adaptation.

    Osteoblasts that become entrapped in bone matrix differentiate into osteocytes. These cells reside in small, fluid-filled lacunae that connect to other lacunae and bone surfaces through adjoining tunnels (ie, canaliculi). Osteocyte cytoplasmic processes traverse the canaliculi and connect to neighboring cells, forming an interconnected network that regulates bone homeostasis via mechanosensory and endocrine cues:

    • Mechanical stress (ie, bone deformation) is sensed by osteocytes via extracellular attachments (eg, integrins) to the canalicular walls, resulting in the release of mediators‚Äîsuch as soluble receptor activator of nuclear factor-kappa B (RANKL) and sclerostin‚Äîthat orchestrate bone remodeling
    • Control of local calcium homeostasis and bone mineralization in response to parathyroid hormone
    • Secretion of fibroblast growth factor 23 (FGF23), the major hormone responsible for regulating serum phosphate levels

    Osteocyte function relies on the presence of gap junctions that connect the cytoplasmic processes between osteocytes. These junctions facilitate cell-to-cell communication, allowing intracellular signals (eg, calcium, cyclic AMP) to propagate to neighboring cells. This osteocyte network is critical for proper control of local osteoclast/osteoblast populations and regulation of regional bony remodeling.

    (Choices A, B, and E) Mesenchymal stem cells within the periosteum, endosteum, and bone marrow give rise to osteochondroprogenitor cells, which can differentiate into osteoblasts (cells that secrete osteoid matrix). Osteoblasts that become trapped in mineralized bone matrix transform into osteocytes, which are terminally differentiated cells that cannot revert back into stem cells or osteoblasts.

    (Choice C) In states that favor bone resorption, osteoblasts, osteocytes, and bone marrow stromal cells increase expression of RANKL. This stimulates the fusion of osteoclast precursor cells (derived from the monocyte linage) into multinucleated osteoclasts that release enzymes causing bone resorption; osteocytes do not fuse into osteoclasts or directly secrete osteolytic enzymes.

    (Choice F) Pericytes are multipotent cells embedded within the microvascular wall. In the bone, they help facilitate fracture healing by regulating local blood flow, promoting angiogenesis, and functioning as an ancillary source for differentiating osteoblasts.

    Educational objective:

    Osteocytes detect increased mechanical load in the bones (eg, weight-bearing exercise) and communicate with one another through gap junctions to orchestrate bone remodeling.
