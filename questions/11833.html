A 66-year-old man is brought to the hospital due to sudden-onset chest pain and nausea. Medical history includes diabetes mellitus. Blood pressure is 70/60 mm Hg and pulse is 60/min. The lungs are clear on auscultation, and the jugular veins are distended. ECG shows ST-segment elevation in leads II, III, and aVF. Chest x-ray is unremarkable. An inferior wall myocardial infarction is diagnosed. Emergency cardiac catheterization reveals complete occlusion of the proximal right coronary artery.     • Explanation

    This patient has an acute myocardial infarction (MI) affecting the inferior wall, which is usually supplied by the right coronary artery. Inferior wall MIs can involve only the left ventricle, both the left and right ventricles, or only the right ventricle. This patient's severe hypotension, prominent jugular venous distension, and clear lungs (ie, absence of pulmonary edema) suggest primarily right ventricular involvement.

    In acute right ventricular MI, contractile dysfunction causes pressure to back up into the right atrium and vena cava, creating elevated central venous pressure (‚Üë CVP) (Choice C). RV output drops, decreasing blood flow through the lungs to the left atrium and creating low pulmonary capillary wedge pressure (‚Üì PCWP). With less blood reaching the left atrium, left ventricular filling decreases and systemic cardiac output drops, causing hypotension and a reflexive increase in systemic vascular resistance (SVR) in an effort to maintain blood pressure.

    These hemodynamic findings of right ventricular MI mimic those seen with obstructive shock involving blockage of blood flow through the pulmonary circulation (eg, pulmonary embolism), and contrast with classic cardiogenic shock resulting from left ventricular contractile dysfunction. In classic cardiogenic shock, blood backs up at the left ventricle, causing increased left atrial pressure (ie, increased PCWP) and pulmonary edema.

    (Choice A) In hypovolemic shock, low blood volume causes low CVP, which leads to low PCWP and cardiac output.

    (Choice D) In septic shock, peripheral vasodilation reduces SVR, and there is a compensatory increase in cardiac output. CVP and PCWP are low due to vascular leakage and low circulating blood volume.

    (Choice E) In high-output heart failure, low SVR triggers an increase in cardiac output in an effort to maintain blood pressure. Despite high output, the heart cannot keep up with the resulting increase in venous return, and elevated PCWP and CVP develop.

    Educational objective:

    Inferior wall myocardial infarction (MI) commonly involves the right ventricle. MI primarily involving the right ventricle is marked by elevated central venous pressure, low pulmonary capillary wedge pressure (due to reduced blood flow through the lungs to the left atrium), and low cardiac output. This contrasts with classic cardiogenic shock, which results from left ventricular failure and leads to elevated pulmonary capillary wedge pressure with pulmonary edema.
