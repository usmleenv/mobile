A 54-year-old man comes to the office for evaluation of hypertension diagnosed 2 years ago. He has had inconsistent follow-up and has not seen a physician in the past 6 months. The patient is not currently taking any medications but feels well and has good exercise tolerance. He has a family history of hypertension and ischemic stroke. BMI is 26 kg/m2. Physical examination is unremarkable. Serum creatinine level is normal. Valsartan and hydrochlorothiazide are prescribed. Blood pressure and plasma renin activity at baseline (before starting treatment) and after 3 weeks of therapy are as follows:

Blood pressure Plasma renin activity, morning
Baseline 165/95 mm Hg 2.5 ng/mL per hour (normal: 1.0-4.0)
After 3 weeks 162/96 mm Hg 2.4 ng/mL per hour

Which of the following best explains the observed findings in this patient?

A. Low-sodium diet
B. Medication nonadherence
C. Obstructive sleep apnea
D. Primary hyperaldosteronism
E. Renal artery stenosis

    • Explanation

    Renin converts angiotensinogen to angiotensin I, which is then converted to angiotensin II, a potent vasoconstrictor that also stimulates aldosterone secretion. Plasma renin activity (PRA) is measured as the amount of angiotensin I generated per unit time and is useful for assessing the function of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS).

    ACE inhibitors (eg, lisinopril) and angiotensin II receptor blockers (eg, losartan, valsartan) inhibit the RAAS pathway, causing a feedback increase in serum renin level. Diuretic-induced hypovolemia leads to decreased renal blood flow, which also causes a feedback increase in renin level. By contrast, drugs that block sympathetic stimulation (eg, beta blockers, clonidine) reduce renin level.

    Because this patient's PRA did not increase as expected after valsartan and hydrochlorothiazide were prescribed, the likely explanation for his follow-up results is medication nonadherence.

    (Choice A) Sodium intake increases intravascular volume, which suppresses renin production via increased blood pressure and increased renal tubular sodium delivery to the macula densa. Conversely, a low-sodium diet usually leads to increased serum renin and aldosterone levels. This patient's normal PRA makes a low-sodium diet unlikely.

    (Choice C) Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is a secondary cause of hypertension that often leads to increased PRA due to a hypoxemia-induced increase in sympathetic activity. OSA is less likely in the setting of an only slightly elevated BMI (ie, 26 kg/m2).

    (Choice D) Primary hyperaldosteronism is a secondary cause of hypertension that involves autonomous and excessive production of aldosterone by the adrenal glands. The high aldosterone level suppresses renin production to cause low PRA.

    (Choice E) Renal artery stenosis is a secondary cause of hypertension that involves reduced renal blood flow leading to elevated PRA.

    Educational objective:

    Plasma renin activity (PRA) is a measure of the amount of angiotensin I generated per unit time. Factors that increase PRA include low sodium intake and antihypertensive medications such as diuretics (eg, hydrochlorothiazide), ACE inhibitors (eg, lisinopril), and angiotensin II receptor blockers (eg, valsartan).
