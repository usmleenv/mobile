    • Explanation

    This patient has the classic triad of decreased respiratory rate, constricted pupils (ie, miosis), and depressed mental status, consistent with acute opioid intoxication. Opioids are derived from the opium poppy plant and act on multiple receptor types (eg, mu, kappa, delta) located throughout the brain, spinal cord, and gastrointestinal tract. In an overdose, overstimulation of these receptors causes numerous adverse effects, the most lethal one being respiratory depression. Respiratory depression is the best predictor of opioid intoxication.

    In addition to the classic triad, other clinical manifestations of opioid intoxication include the following:

    • Hypothermia: Opioid receptors play a role in thermoregulation, and their activation can impair thermogenesis. In addition, intoxicated patients are often obtunded and are incapable of protecting themselves from exposure to the environment, leading to heat loss.
    • Hypotension (usually mild): Opioids induce histamine release from mast cells. Histamine binding to H1 receptors on vascular endothelial cells results in arterial vasodilation.
    • Decreased tidal volume: Opioid binding at receptors in the brainstem respiratory center causes depression of ventilation as well as inhibition of the protective ventilatory response to hypercapnia and hypoxia. This overall depressed ventilatory drive results in both reduced respiratory rate and decreased tidal volume.

    Decreased bowels sounds and constipation are also common, as opioids slow gastric emptying and decrease peristalsis.

    Educational objective:

    The classic triad of opioid intoxication is decreased respiratory rate, pupillary constriction (ie, miosis), and depressed mental status. Hypothermia, hypotension, and decreased tidal volume are also common findings.
