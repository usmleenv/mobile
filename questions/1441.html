    This patient with a mutation in the neutrophil oxidase enzyme has chronic granulomatous disease (CGD), a condition that results in recurrent bacterial and fungal infections due to impaired intracellular killing by phagocytes.

    CGD is caused by a genetic defect in the NADPH oxidase complex. NADPH oxidase normally functions to transfer an electron from NADPH to oxygen, resulting in the production of superoxide (O2‚Ä¢‚àí) and other reactive oxygen species such as hydroxyl radicals (HO‚Ä¢) and hydrogen peroxide (H2O2). These oxidants activate granule proteases (eg, elastase, cathepsin G) that destroy engulfed pathogens and also have direct microbicidal activity.

    Most microorganisms produce H2O2 as a waste product of metabolism, which can help activate microbicidal peptides within defective neutrophils in patients with CGD. However, some pathogens also produce catalase, which can destroy their own metabolically produced H2O2. As a result, the bacteria and fungi responsible for infections in CGD are usually catalase-positive organisms, particularly the following:

    • Staphylococcus aureus
    • Burkholderia cepacia
    • Serratia marcescens
    • Nocardia
    • Aspergillus

    (Choices B and E) Catalase-negative organisms (eg, Enterococcus faecalis, Streptococcus pyogenes) cannot prevent the accumulation of H2O2 within phagosomes, a process that allows the phagocytes to generate potent microbicidal agents (eg, hypochlorite) and activate granule proteases even when host O2‚Ä¢‚àí production is impaired.

    (Choices C and D) Patients with CGD are at increased risk of bacterial and fungal infections, not parasitic (Giardia lamblia) or viral (herpes simplex virus type 1) infections.

    Educational objective:

    Chronic granulomatous disease (CGD) results from defective NADPH oxidase. Normally, this enzyme participates in the respiratory burst pathway to produce reactive oxygen species critical in intracellular pathogen killing. Patients with CGD develop recurrent bacterial and fungal infections predominantly caused by 5 catalase-positive organisms: Staphylococcus aureus, Burkholderia cepacia, Serratia marcescens, Nocardia, and Aspergillus.
