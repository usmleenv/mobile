A 66-year-old man comes to the office for hypertension follow-up. The patient takes lisinopril and amlodipine, but his blood pressure recordings have been 140-150/85-95 mm Hg over the past 2 office visits. He has had no chest pain or shortness of breath. The patient also reports worsening urinary symptoms over the past year that include hesitancy, straining during urination, poor urinary flow, and waking up once or twice a night to urinate. The patient has no other medical conditions and does not use tobacco, alcohol, or recreational drugs. Blood pressure is 142/88 mm Hg and pulse is 70/min. Physical examination shows a mildly enlarged, smooth prostate but is otherwise unremarkable. Serum chemistry studies and urinalysis are normal. Which of the following medications would be most effective for treating both of this patient's medical conditions?

A. Doxazosin
B. Finasteride
C. Hydralazine
D. Hydrochlorothiazide
E. Metoprolol

    • Explanation

    This patient's obstructive urinary symptoms and mildly enlarged prostate are consistent with benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH). In addition, he has hypertension that is not well controlled.

    Alpha-1 blockers are widely used for BPH and work by relaxing smooth muscle in the bladder neck and prostate, opening the bladder outlet and decreasing the resistance to the flow of urine. In addition, nonuroselective alpha-1 blockers (eg, doxazosin, prazosin, terazosin) relax smooth muscle tone in arterial walls, decreasing vascular resistance and blood pressure. Uroselective alpha-1 blockers (specific for alpha-1A subtype; eg, tamsulosin) primarily affect the urinary tract smooth muscles and have less effect on blood pressure.

    Although alpha-1 blockers are not first-line medications for hypertension, they can be useful second-line agents in patients who require treatment for comorbid BPH. Using a single drug that can address multiple issues can potentially minimize adverse effects and drug interactions.

    (Choice B) Finasteride is a 5-alpha reductase inhibitor that reduces the conversion of testosterone to dihydrotestosterone, the hormone that drives glandular enlargement in BPH. Finasteride can decrease prostate volume and reduce the restriction of urinary flow but has no significant effect on blood pressure.

    (Choice C) Hydralazine is a direct vasodilator that works by relaxing smooth muscles in the arterial walls. It would not be useful in the treatment of BPH.

    (Choice D) Thiazide diuretics (eg, hydrochlorothiazide) inhibit the Na+-Cl‚àí cotransporter in the distal convoluted tubule, leading to diuresis and natriuresis. They may also have a minor vasodilatory effect. Thiazides are widely used for hypertension but have no benefit in the treatment of BPH.

    (Choice E) Metoprolol is a selective beta-1 receptor blocker that has negative chronotropic and inotropic effects on the heart; it is used to treat hypertension and coronary artery disease. Beta-1 receptor blockers do not help with BPH symptoms.

    Educational objective:

    Alpha-1 blockers reduce resistance to urine flow in the bladder outlet and can improve symptoms in benign prostatic hyperplasia. In addition, nonuroselective alpha-1 blockers (eg, doxazosin, prazosin, terazosin) lower blood pressure and are therefore particularly useful in patients with comorbid hypertension.
