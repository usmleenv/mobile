A 7-year-old boy is brought to the office for evaluation of a neck mass. A week ago, the patient was taken to an urgent care clinic for nasal congestion and sore throat. The symptoms resolved after a few days, but his parents noticed a persistent neck swelling. The patient has no prior medical conditions and immunizations are up to date. On physical examination, he appears active and playful and is well nourished. Neck examination shows a mildly tender, 2-cm, anterior midline mass that moves upward when the patient swallows. Which of the following is the most likely cause of this patient's lesion?

A. Abnormal migration of neural crest cells
B. Cervical implantation of thymic tissue
C. Dilation of cervical lymphatic channels
D. Incomplete obliteration of a duct
E. Persistence of second branchial cleft structures
F. Trapping of skin structures along embryonic fusion lines

    • Explanation

    This patient has a thyroglossal duct cyst (TDC), which classically presents as a midline neck mass that moves superiorly with tongue protrusion or swallowing. A TDC may be identified incidentally during routine examination but is often first noticed when it becomes secondarily infected (eg, erythema, swelling, tenderness) after an upper respiratory infection.

    In utero, the thyroid gland forms from an outpouching of pharyngeal epithelium at the base of the tongue. It then descends to the base of the anterior neck via the thyroglossal duct, which extends from the foramen cecum at the base of the tongue to the superior border of the isthmus of the thyroid. Normally, the duct then atrophies.

    Failed atrophy of the thyroglossal duct can result in a TDC from the epithelial remnants within the duct. Because the tract connects to the tongue, tongue protrusion or swallowing causes the TDC to move superiorly. Ectopic thyroid tissue may also be present and can form anywhere along the duct's path.

    (Choice A) Abnormal migration of neural crest cells can cause paragangliomas, neuroendocrine tumors that develop from extraadrenal, autonomic paraganglia. They most commonly arise from the carotid body and typically present as a lateral neck mass.

    (Choice B) Cervical implantation of thymic tissue is a rare cause of cystic neck mass. Ectopic thymic cysts can arise anywhere along the thymus' descent from the angle of the jaw to the mediastinum, but when they form in the neck they are most commonly found on the left side deep to the sternocleidomastoid muscle.

    (Choice C) Cystic hygromas are fluid-filled sacs of dilated lymphatic vessels that result from abnormal lymphatic development. They present at birth as a soft mass at the posterior or lateral aspects of the neck, not the anterior midline.

    (Choice E) Branchial cleft cysts are embryologic remnants of branchial cleft structures that often present as a neck mass after becoming secondarily infected. However, they are located laterally (anterior to the sternocleidomastoid muscle), not midline, and do not move with swallowing.

    (Choice F) Trapped skin structures (eg, hair follicles, sebaceous glands) along embryonic fusion lines result in formation of dermoid cysts within subcutaneous tissue. They may be located in the midline (eg, submental region, neck), but would not rise with swallowing.

    Educational objective:

    Failed atrophy of the thyroglossal duct in utero can result in thyroglossal duct cysts that can be located anywhere along the path of thyroid descent (from the tongue base to the thyroid isthmus). They usually present as an anterior midline neck mass that moves superiorly with swallowing or tongue protrusion.
