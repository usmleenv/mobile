A 54-year-old man comes to the emergency department due to intermittent fever, headache, body aches, and fatigue for the past week. He has taken over-the-counter flu medicines, but the symptoms continue to persist. The patient is a petroleum engineer; 6 months ago, he spent 8 weeks in Brazil developing a new oil field. Temperature is 37.6 C (99.7 F), blood pressure is 128/72 mm Hg, pulse is 102/min, and respirations are 18/min. On physical examination, the lungs are clear to auscultation and there are no cardiac murmurs. The spleen is palpable 2 cm below the left costal margin. The remainder of the examination is unremarkable. Laboratory results are as follows:

Hemoglobin 8.8 g/dL
Platelets 96,000/mm3
Leukocytes 7,200/mm3

Peripheral blood smear is shown in the exhibit.

Which of the following is the most likely underlying cause of this patient's current condition?

A. Water-borne protozoa
B. Hypnozoite-forming parasite
C. Mosquito-transmitted virus
D. Soil-derived dimorphic fungus
E. Toxin-induced oncogenic mutation

    • Explanation

    This patient visited Brazil and subsequently developed a febrile illness with anemia, thrombocytopenia, and an intraerythrocytic ring-shaped parasite, indicating malaria. Four major Plasmodium species cause malaria (Plasmodium falciparum, Plasmodium vivax, Plasmodium ovale, Plasmodium malariae), but most cases worldwide are due to P falciparum or P vivax. In Brazil, the dominant strain is P vivax (~90%).

    Although the overall life cycle of malaria is relatively preserved across species, P vivax and P ovale have a unique feature not seen in the other strains: They undergo a dormant hepatic phase (hypnozoite stage) that may cause recurrent parasitemia weeks or months after initial symptoms. Manifestations of recurrence resemble those of initial disease; most patients develop high fever, flu-like symptoms (eg, myalgia, fatigue, headache, chills), and splenomegaly. Laboratory evaluation typically shows anemia, mild thrombocytopenia, and indirect bilirubinemia. The diagnosis is confirmed when trophozoites (intraerythrocytic, ringed inclusions) are seen on peripheral blood smear using Giemsa stain.

    Although P falciparum can be treated with an agent that targets only the intraerythrocytic phase of infection, P vivax and P ovale must be treated with a combination of drugs that targets the intraerythrocytic phase (eg, chloroquine) and the dormant hepatic phase (eg, primaquine) to ensure clearance. Otherwise, patients are at high risk for relapsed symptoms weeks or months later (as in this case).

    (Choice A) Entamoeba histolytica is a common parasitic infection spread via contaminated drinking water. Most infections are asymptomatic, but a minority present with colitis; invasive disease develops in <1% of patients and is most often marked by amoebic liver abscess. Anemia, thrombocytopenia, and intraerythrocytic parasites are not seen.

    (Choice C) Dengue fever and chikungunya fever are viral illnesses transmitted by mosquitos. Dengue causes hemorrhagic febrile syndrome, and chikungunya usually causes fever with severe joint pain. Neither has ring-shaped intraerythrocytic forms.

    (Choice D) Histoplasmosis is a dimorphic fungus found in soil contaminated by avian droppings. Most cases in healthy adults are asymptomatic, but a minority develop manifestations of community-acquired pneumonia (eg, lobar infiltrate, fever, cough). Intraerythrocytic ring-shaped forms are not seen.

    (Choice E) Toxin-induced oncogenic transformation could lead to hematologic malignancy or myelodysplasia, which could cause alterations to erythrocytes (eg, teardrop cells), anemia, and splenomegaly. However, ring-shaped erythrocyte inclusions are not seen.

    Educational objective:

    Unlike Plasmodium falciparum, the eradication of Plasmodium vivax and Plasmodium ovale requires primaquine to target the dormant hepatic phase. Patients not receiving treatment that targets the hepatic phase will likely develop relapsed symptoms weeks or months later.
