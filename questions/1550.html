A 24-year-old man comes to the clinic due to 2 days of painful blisters on the shaft of his penis. The patient has had 5 lifetime sexual partners and has recently been sexually active with a woman. He uses condoms inconsistently. Examination shows multiple vesicular lesions on the penis. Tzanck smear is positive for multinucleated giant cells. HIV testing is negative. The patient has had 4 similar episodes over the past 2 years but has been too embarrassed to seek medical care until now. Which of the following treatments would most likely have prevented this patient's recurrent symptoms?

A. Daily valacyclovir continued indefinitely
B. Immunoglobulin during the first episode
C. Lamivudine with recurrence of blisters
D. Regular condom use after the first episode
E. Weeklong course of acyclovir during the first episode

    This patient has vesicular lesions and a positive Tzanck smear indicating genital herpes simplex virus (HSV). Most patients experience recurrent, episodic symptoms due to reactivation of latent HSV within the S2, S3, and S4 dorsal root (sensory) ganglia. However, outbreaks can be prevented or minimized with daily oral valacyclovir (preferred because of its once-daily dosing and good bioavailability), acyclovir, or famciclovir. Although antiviral agents do not eliminate latent virus forms, they suppress viral multiplication when reactivation occurs. Therefore, patients with frequent recurrences are usually offered daily viral suppressive therapy. Because this treatment is effective and has minimal adverse effects, it is generally continued indefinitely to prevent recurrent symptoms.

    (Choice B) Immunoglobulin therapy can be used for varicella postexposure prophylaxis (eg, in neonates or pregnant women) but is not commonly used for genital HSV infection.

    (Choice C) Lamivudine is a nucleoside analog reverse transcriptase inhibitor with activity against HIV and hepatitis B virus. It is not known to be effective against HSV.

    (Choice D) Although condom use may prevent sexual transmission of HSV (and therefore might have prevented this patient's primary HSV infection), it does not reduce the frequency of recurrence of genital herpes once infection has occurred.

    (Choice E) A short (7-10 days) course of oral acyclovir during primary genital HSV infection reduces the duration of viral shedding, time for lesion healing, and local pain; however, it does not appear to alter recurrence rates.

    Educational objective:

    A genital vesicular rash and a positive Tzanck smear suggest genital herpes simplex virus (HSV) infection. Recurrence is common and can be mitigated with daily oral valacyclovir (preferred), acyclovir, or famciclovir.
