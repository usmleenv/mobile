A 61-year-old man comes to the office due to a painful rash on his torso for the past 3 days. The patient has a history of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease with frequent exacerbations, which are treated with systemic glucocorticoids and bronchodilators. He is a retired schoolteacher and lives with his wife. Skin examination findings are shown in the exhibit.

The remainder of the physical examination shows no abnormalities. Which of the following events most likely immediately preceded the appearance of skin rash in this patient?

A. Alteration in the patient's normal skin flora
B. Close physical contact with an infected person
C. Formation of anti-epidermal autoantibodies
D. Inhalation of pathogen-containing aerosols
E. Reactivation of latent pathogen in neural ganglia
F. Waning of organism-specific antibodies

    • Explanation

    This patient has a painful, crusted vesicular rash on his torso, likely indicating shingles (herpes zoster). Shingles is caused by reactivation of varicella-zoster virus (VZV), a double-stranded DNA virus of the herpesvirus family.

    Primary VZV infection occurs most commonly in childhood. Inoculation in the nasopharyngeal mucosa is followed by migration of the virus to the regional lymphatics. Subsequent dermatotrophic migration produces a diffuse vesicular rash associated with fever, malaise, and pharyngitis (chickenpox). Then, the virus migrates via sensory nerves to cranial nerve and dorsal spinal ganglia, where it lies dormant for decades.

    Most patients who have had primary chickenpox do not develop recurrent disseminated disease. Over time, however, waning cell-mediated immunity allows reactivation of the virus, which typically spreads down a single spinal nerve to cause a painful, erythematous, vesicular rash in a dermatomal distribution. The primary risk factor for reactivation is increasing age; other factors can include malignancy, autoimmune disease, physical trauma, and immune-suppressing therapy (eg, glucocorticoids).

    (Choice A) Colonization of the skin by transient flora (eg, Staphylococcus aureus, Candida species, gram-negative bacilli) occasionally leads to clinical disease, especially following antibiotic exposure (eg, candidiasis). However, VZV does not remain on the surface of the skin for prolonged periods.

    (Choices B and D) Primary VZV infection (ie, chickenpox) can be acquired by direct contact or airborne aerosol from patients with active chickenpox or zoster lesions. However, primary VZV infection causes diffuse lesions; lesions in a dermatomal distribution are seen only with reactivation from sensory ganglia.

    (Choice C) Anti-epidermal antibodies are a feature of dermatis herpetiformis, a rare autoimmune disease that occurs as a consequence of gluten sensitivity. Although patients develop areas of grouped papules or vesicles, multiple regions (eg, elbows, forearms, knees, scalp, back) tend to be affected and to be intensely pruritic. This patient with advanced age, exposure to glucocorticoids, and a single area of painful vesicles is far more likely to have shingles.

    (Choice F) Declining VZV-specific immunity increases the risk of reactivation. However, the immune response to VZV is primarily cell-mediated, rather than humoral (ie, antibodies).

    Educational objective:

    Primary varicella-zoster virus infection (chickenpox) occurs most commonly in childhood. The virus then migrates to the sensory ganglia, where it lies dormant for decades. Impairments in cell-mediated immunity (eg, advanced age, HIV, immunosuppressive medications) can lead to reactivation of the virus, which typically spreads down a single nerve to cause a painful, erythematous, vesicular rash in a dermatomal distribution.
