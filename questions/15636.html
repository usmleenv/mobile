A 27-year-old man comes to the emergency department with progressive right knee swelling and pain. He has no history of trauma to the area. The patient has no other medical conditions and takes no medications. X-rays reveal a large lytic and sclerotic lesion involving the proximal tibia, with extensive soft tissue swelling. After additional confirmatory testing, the patient undergoes a right-sided, above-knee amputation. Histologic examination of the resected mass is shown in the exhibit.

Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Chondrosarcoma
B. Ewing sarcoma
C. Metastatic adenocarcinoma
D. Osteoid osteoma
E. Osteosarcoma

    • Explanation

    Histopathology of this patient's proximal tibial lesion is consistent with osteosarcoma, the most common primary malignant bone tumor in children and young adults. Most cases arise in the metaphyses of long bones (eg, proximal tibia, distal femur), near the growth plate where bone proliferation is the highest.

    Patients typically have localized pain and soft tissue swelling. X-ray often reveals a lytic and sclerotic bone lesion (due to destruction of existing bone and production of neoplastic bone) that extends into the surrounding soft tissue. Periosteal elevation (ie, Codman triangle) and a sunburst periosteal reaction are also common.

    Osteosarcomas are thought to arise from mesenchymal stem cells or osteoblastic progenitor cells. Histopathology shows malignant cells (eg, pleomorphic, hyperchromatic nuclei, prominent nucleoli) that produce neoplastic osteoid (ie, unmineralized bone matrix composed of type I collagen) and thin trabeculae of mineralized bone. Treatment typically includes surgical resection and chemotherapy.

    (Choice A) Chondrosarcoma is a malignant, cartilage-forming tumor that typically occurs in the axial skeleton (eg, pelvis) of patients age >40. Unlike osteosarcoma, it does not produce neoplastic osteoid or bone. Histopathology shows neoplastic chondrocytes in a cartilage matrix that invades the existing bone.

    (Choice B) Ewing sarcoma, the second most common primary bone malignancy in children and adolescents, often arises in the diaphysis of long bones, causing progressive pain, swelling, and lytic bone lesions. Histopathology shows sheets of small, round, blue cells; no neoplastic osteoid or bone is produced.

    (Choice C) Adenocarcinoma of the lung often metastasizes to bone. However, histopathology shows glands lined by malignant cells that produce mucin.

    (Choice D) Osteoid osteoma is a small, benign, bone-forming tumor that typically occurs in the lower extremity of adolescent boys, causing pain that is characteristically worse at night. Histopathology shows irregular woven bone lined by a single layer of benign-appearing osteoblasts (not pleomorphic, hyperchromatic cells).

    Educational objective:

    Osteosarcoma is the most common primary malignant bone tumor in children and young adults and typically arises near the metaphyses of long bones (eg, femur, tibia), causing progressive pain and soft tissue swelling. X-ray typically reveals a lytic and sclerotic bone lesion, and histopathology shows malignant tumor cells that produce neoplastic osteoid and mineralized bone.
