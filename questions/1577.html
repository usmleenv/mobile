A 21-year-old man comes to the office after noticing that his urine has recently had a frothy appearance. The patient has also had fatigue and anorexia. He has no chronic medical conditions but has been taking nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs after a knee injury during soccer practice several months ago. Temperature is 36.9 C (98.5 F), blood pressure is 115/68 mm Hg, pulse is 60/min, and respirations are 14/min. Physical examination reveals symmetric pitting edema of the ankles. Which of the following is most likely decreased in this patient?

A. Aldosterone production
B. Antidiuretic hormone release
C. Plasma oncotic pressure
D. Renal sodium reabsorption
E. Systemic capillary permeability
F. Tissue lymphatic drainage

    • Explanation

    This patient's frothy urine is concerning for heavy proteinuria and results from the soap-like (ie, surfactant) effect of albumin. In combination with peripheral edema, this suggests nephrotic syndrome (likely due to membranous nephropathy from nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug use). The primary driver of edema formation in nephrotic syndrome is increased renal sodium reabsorption (Choice D); this is triggered by independent processes known as intravascular underfilling and overfilling.

    Underfilling results from the characteristic hypoalbuminemia in nephrotic syndrome that leads to decreased plasma oncotic pressure. This increases net capillary filtration pressure and causes fluid to leak into the interstitial space. The resulting intravascular volume depletion causes renal hypoperfusion, stimulating the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) to increase aldosterone production (Choice A). Therefore, sodium retention occurs as a compensatory response to proteinuria (ie, secondary sodium retention).

    Underfilling alone does not fully explain edema formation because accumulation of protein-free fluid in the interstitium decreases interstitial oncotic pressure (limiting the transcapillary pressure gradient). An additional mechanism contributing to edema is overfilling, which is characterized by primary defects in renal sodium handling (eg, aberrant filtration of plasma proteases that activate epithelial sodium channels). These changes promote intravascular volume expansion independently of RAAS activation (ie, primary sodium retention).

    In many patients, both underfilling and overfilling can occur, the degree of which varies depending on the stage of nephrotic syndrome and the underlying etiology.

    (Choice B) Antidiuretic hormone increases in nephrotic syndrome in an attempt to maintain serum osmolality in the setting of increased sodium retention. The resultant free water retention contributes to edema formation.

    (Choice E) Increased systemic capillary permeability may play a minor role in edema formation in nephrotic syndrome for poorly understood reasons. However, decreased systemic capillary permeability would not cause edema.

    (Choice F) Decreased tissue lymphatic drainage can cause peripheral edema, but the rate of lymphatic drainage increases in nephrotic syndrome to compensate for interstitial fluid accumulation. In the early stages of nephrotic syndrome, this compensatory mechanism minimizes significant peripheral edema; however, lymphatic drainage capacity eventually becomes overwhelmed.

    Educational objective:

    Edema formation in nephrotic syndrome occurs due to a combination of intravascular underfilling and overfilling. Underfilling results from hypoalbuminemia, which decreases plasma oncotic pressure and results in sodium retention (ie, secondary sodium retention). Overfilling results from sodium retention due to primary defects in renal sodium handling (ie, primary sodium retention).
