A 50-year-old man is brought to the emergency department due to chest pain. He passed out briefly after the pain started. Medical history includes hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and early-stage chronic kidney disease. The patient reports that his father had a heart attack at a young age. Despite treatment, the patient dies during hospitalization. An autopsy is performed. Light microscopy of the aortic wall is shown on the slide below.

Which of the following underlying mechanisms is most likely responsible for this patient's disease process?

A. Atherosclerosis
B. Calciphylaxis
C. Cystic degeneration of the media
D. Granulomatous inflammation
E. Monckeberg sclerosis

    • Explanation

    The above autopsy image shows cystic medial degeneration (necrosis), the classic histologic finding in aortic dissection. The tunica media is the largest and strongest of the 3 portions of the aortic wall. With aging, collagen, elastin, and smooth muscle in the aortic media are broken down and replaced by a mucoid extracellular matrix. There is elastic tissue fragmentation in a "basket weave" pattern with development of many cystic collections of mucopolysaccharide. These changes result in progressive loss of integrity, reducing the vessel's ability to handle wall stress from conditions such as systemic hypertension.

    Once cystic medial degeneration develops, a small intimal tear can readily extend into and throughout the media, creating a lengthy dissection plane that propagates proximally or distally. As blood enters the dissection plane a false aortic lumen is created and aortic branches (eg, left subclavian artery, renal arteries) may become obstructed. The enlarging hematoma in the false lumen can eventually penetrate the adventitia, resulting in full-thickness rupture.

    (Choice A) Atherosclerosis primarily affects the intima and is an important contributor to acute coronary syndrome. Histologically it appears as an atheroma with an endothelial covering, fibrous cap, and necrotic core. Atherosclerosis can contribute to cystic medial degeneration but is not the primary pathophysiologic mechanism.

    (Choice B) Calcific uremic arteriolopathy (calciphylaxis) can occur with advanced chronic kidney disease and leads to severe skin ischemia and necrosis. Histologically, it is characterized by superficial arteriolar calcification, subintimal fibrosis, and thrombosis. It does not typically affect large vessels such as the aorta.

    (Choice D) Granulomatous inflammation of large vessels can occur in Takayasu arteritis and tuberculous aortitis. Histopathology demonstrates granuloma formation in the media with multinucleated giant cells.

    (Choice E) Monckeberg sclerosis is a rare disease that results in diffuse medial calcification of small and medium-sized arteries. Calcification typically appears as blue-to-violet irregular deposits on hematoxylin‚Äìeosin stains, as seen in this calcified atherosclerotic plaque.

    Educational objective:

    Cystic medial degeneration (necrosis) is the classic histologic finding in aortic dissection, as it weakens the aortic wall and allows a small intimal tear to readily propagate. Collagen, elastin, and smooth muscle are replaced by a basophilic mucoid extracellular matrix with elastic tissue fragmentation and cystic collections of mucopolysaccharide.
