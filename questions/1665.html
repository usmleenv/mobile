A patient on comfort care in the hospital passes away due to natural causes. The body is taken to the morgue for autopsy. The pathologist begins an examination the next day and notes that the skeletal muscles across the patient's body are significantly stiffened. Failure of which of the following processes most strongly contributes to this phenomenon?

A. Calcium binding to troponin-C
B. Cross-bridge formation
C. Myosin head detachment from the actin filament
D. Myosin light chain phosphorylation by a specific enzyme
E. Tropomyosin displacement from the groove on the actin molecule

    • Explanation

    This recently deceased patient demonstrates rigor mortis, in which failure of muscle filaments to disengage results in persistent, diffuse muscle stiffening.

    Skeletal muscle cells contract (shorten in length) using sarcomeres, functional units containing actin and myosin filaments. Filament interaction and overlap are mediated through a calcium- and ATP-driven process known as cross-bridge cycling:

    • At rest, the myosin head is charged by ADP and inorganic phosphate (Pi) from previously bound and hydrolyzed ATP. However, calcium-modulated regulatory complexes of troponin-C and tropomyosin prevent filament interaction by occluding binding sites on actin. The sarcoplasmic reticulum actively resorbs calcium at rest through an ATP-driven channel (SERCA).
    • Depolarization of skeletal muscle allows the SR to release calcium. This stimulates troponin-tropomyosin complexes to open access, allowing the myosin head to perpendicularly bind actin (cross-bridge formation).
    • The bound head releases Pi and bends the cross-bridge 45¬∞, increasing filament overlap and shortening the sarcomere length. Once the process is completed, ADP is released.
    • A new ATP molecule must bind the myosin head to dissolve the cross-bridge, returning the head to its original position to restart the cycle.
    • Cycling continues while both calcium and ATP are readily available; continued cycling across multiple heads further increases actin-myosin overlap, increasing or maintaining contraction. Once the stimulation for contraction dissipates, calcium is resorbed by the sarcoplasmic reticulum and the muscle relaxes.

    After death occurs and respiration ceases, ATP stores quickly deplete. Intracellular calcium levels rapidly rise without ATP maintaining sarcoplasmic storage, triggering cross-bridge formation. Since cross-bridges cannot dissolve without ATP, this results in muscle stiffening that spreads across the deceased patient's body during the first 12 hours after death.

    (Choices A, B, and E) Calcium binding to troponin-C shifts tropomyosin away from myosin binding sites, facilitating cross-bridge formation. Failure of these processes prevents muscle contraction.

    (Choice D) Smooth muscle cells use myosin light chain kinase to phosphorylate the myosin head to trigger contraction. However, myosin head phosphorylation is not necessary for skeletal muscle contraction.

    Educational objective:

    Muscle contraction involves continuous actin-myosin cross-bridge cycling driven by release of sarcoplasmic calcium stores. After death, loss of ATP prevents both myosin head detachment and clearance of cytoplasmic calcium, leading to diffuse and persistent skeletal muscle stiffening (rigor mortis).
