A 26-year-old man comes to the office due to 2 days of rash on his right thigh. It is mildly pruritic and not painful. He has no chronic medical conditions but occasionally takes ibuprofen for shoulder pain following intensive workouts at the gym.     This patient's annular, erythematous rash with central clearing suggests erythema migrans (EM), the hallmark feature of early localized Lyme disease. Lyme disease is caused by the spirochete Borrelia burgdorferi and is transmitted by the Ixodes scapularis tick during blood feeding. B burgdorferi replicates at the site of inoculation, which generates a rash 7-14 days after transmission in most patients.

    EM typically begins as a confluent macule that expands outward as B burgdorferi spreads through the dermis. Most cases evolve over days into an annular lesion with central clearing, which resembles a bull's-eye. Pain is rare, and pruritis is typically mild or absent. Some patients have systemic manifestations (eg, malaise, fever, headache). Because many patients with Lyme disease do not recall a tick bite, suspicion for Lyme disease should be raised in any individual from an endemic area who develops a characteristic EM rash. Although this patient's location is not known, a spreading annular rash with central clearing is highly suggestive of Lyme disease.

    (Choice A) Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (eg, ibuprofen) can sometimes cause a fixed drug reaction, which is marked by the activation of cytotoxic T cells in a particular location within the dermis. Although patients frequently develop an annular rash, it is usually a dusky red plaque with no central clearing.

    (Choice B) Tinea corporis can sometimes be spread through contact with fomites (eg, gym equipment). Although this fungal infection often causes an annular lesion with central clearing that spreads outward, the advancing edge is often scaly with raised borders, and the lesions are usually intensely pruritic.

    (Choice C) A type 1 hypersensitivity (allergic) reaction to an insect bite usually arises within minutes and is marked by an edematous lesion that is intensely pruritic. This patient with 2 days of progressive rash that is minimally pruritic is unlikely to be experiencing an allergic reaction.

    (Choice E) Streptococcal lymphangitis is marked by the formation of erythematous streaks up the lymphatic channel. The formation of an annular lesion with central clearing would be atypical.

    Educational objective:

    Lyme disease should be considered in any individual who develops erythema migrans, a slowly expanding annular lesion with central clearing (bull's-eye rash). Most cases are not pruritic or painful, but patients sometimes have systemic symptoms (eg, fever, myalgia, headache).
