A 64-year-old man comes to the emergency department due to fever, anorexia, and nausea. He recently immigrated to the United States from Vietnam to live with his son. Several weeks ago, the patient was found to have a positive tuberculin skin test after a routine office visit. He had no respiratory symptoms, and a chest x-ray was unremarkable. A month ago, he started isoniazid therapy with pyridoxine supplementation. Today, his temperature is 38.3 C (100.9 F). On physical examination, there is mild abdominal discomfort. Laboratory studies are pending. Which of the following is the most likely cause of this patient's current symptoms?

A. Factitious disorder
B. Gastric mucosal damage
C. Hepatocyte damage
D. Peripheral nerve damage
E. Serum sickness

    • Explanation

    This patient's positive tuberculin skin test, lack of respiratory symptoms, and normal chest x-ray indicate latent tuberculosis infection (LTBI). Treatment for LTBI typically involves a rifamycin-based regimen or isoniazid monotherapy (with pyridoxine added for those at high risk of peripheral neuropathy). This patient was treated with isoniazid and pyridoxine; he now returns a month later with fever, anorexia, and nausea, suggesting a medication side-effect.

    INH is directly hepatotoxic. In 10%-20% of patients, INH causes acute, mild hepatic dysfunction, characterized by transient increases in serum aminotransferases (AST/SGOT, ALT/SGPT). This adverse effect usually occurs during the first 4-6 months of treatment, and, in most cases, liver function tests return to baseline with continued INH therapy. In rare instances (<1%), INH induces a frank hepatitis that causes a syndrome similar to viral hepatitis (eg, fever, anorexia, nausea, jaundice); this sometimes evolves into a severe form with progressive liver dysfunction and death.

    (Choice A) Factitious disorder is the intentional falsification or inducement of symptoms with the goal of assuming the sick role. It is unlikely in this patient, who should be evaluated for INH hepatotoxicity.

    (Choice B) Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs can cause gastric mucosal damage by inhibiting gastric prostaglandin production.

    (Choice D) Peripheral neuropathy is the other major side effect of INH therapy. Patients at high risk for peripheral neuropathy (eg, pregnancy, HIV, diabetes mellitus) are given supplemental pyridoxine, which significantly reduces risk. Pyridoxine does not alter risk of hepatitis, and peripheral neuropathy does not present with nausea, fever, and anorexia.

    (Choice E) Serum sickness drug hypersensitivity generally causes fever, urticaria, arthralgias, proteinuria, and lymphadenopathy 5-10 days after exposure to the drug (antigen). This patient does not have arthralgias or skin findings.

    Educational objective:

    Isoniazid can be directly hepatotoxic, causing acute, mild hepatic dysfunction in 10%-20% of patients and frank hepatitis (fever, anorexia, and nausea, sometimes progressing to hepatic failure) in a small percentage of patients.
