A 12-year-old girl is brought to the office for evaluation of short stature. She was an average-sized infant, but, over the past few years, her height-growth velocity has plateaued. The patient has not menstruated. Menarche occurred in her mother at age 14, and both her parents are tall. The patient's height is at <2nd percentile and weight is at the 50th percentile for age and sex. She has no breast buds. She has a low hairline, a short and wide neck, a broad chest, and widely spaced nipples. Which of the following is the most likely underlying mechanism for this patient's condition?

A. Balanced autosomal translocation
B. Frameshift mutation
C. Meiotic nondisjunction
D. Skewed X chromosome inactivation
E. Uniparental disomy

    • Explanation

    This patient with short stature (height <3rd percentile) has delayed puberty (ie, no breast development by age 12) and dysmorphic features (eg, low hairline, short/wide neck, widely spaced nipples). This constellation of findings is characteristic of Turner syndrome (TS), a sex chromosome disorder due to loss of an X chromosome. Because the X chromosome contains a short stature homeobox (SHOX) gene, which normally promotes long bone growth, patients often present with declining linear velocity, as seen in this girl.

    Aneuploidy (abnormal chromosome number), as in TS, is most often caused by meiotic nondisjunction during gametogenesis. Meiotic nondisjunction refers to failed separation of homologous chromosomes (meiosis I) or sister chromatids (meiosis II), which results in a gamete with an abnormal number of chromosomes (eg, 22, 24). When a gamete lacking an X chromosome is fertilized with a gamete containing a normal number of chromosomes, the embryo has a 45,X genotype.

    Alternately, absence of an X chromosome may be due to mitotic nondisjunction after fertilization. This results in somatic mosaicism, in which the X chromosome is missing in only some cells (45,X/46,XX), and a milder TS phenotype.

    (Choice A) Balanced translocations are clinically silent because genetic material is exchanged between nonhomologous chromosomes but not lost. Affected individuals can have offspring with unbalanced translocations (eg, trisomy 21). However, autosomal translocation would not cause Turner's syndrome.

    (Choice B) Frameshift mutation occurs when a number of nucleotides not evenly divisible by 3 are inserted into or deleted from a sequence of coding DNA. The reading frame is shifted during translation, creating a different peptide sequence.

    (Choice D) Skewed X-chromosome inactivation occurs when one X chromosome is inactivated more often than would occur by chance alone. However, because many of the genes that contribute to TS (eg, SHOX) normally escape X inactivation, findings of TS are not typical with this mechanism.

    (Choice E) Uniparental disomy occurs when both copies of a chromosome are inherited from 1 parent. Disease occurs when the inherited chromosomes contain genes that are physiologically imprinted (silenced), and gene expression normally relies on the other parent's genes (which are absent), as in Prader-Willi/Angelman syndrome.

    Educational objective:

    Turner syndrome is characterized by loss of an X chromosome, most often due to meiotic nondisjunction during gametogenesis.
