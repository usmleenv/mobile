A 34-year-old woman comes to the emergency department due to 6 days of fatigue and intermittent fever. Medical history is significant for depression. She does not use tobacco, alcohol, or recreational drugs and has no medication allergies. The patient lives in New York City and recently traveled to central Africa, where she helped establish a nutrition program. Blood microscopy findings are shown in the exhibit.

Urine pregnancy test is negative. Which of the following is the best treatment for this patient's current condition?

A. Amphotericin B
B. Artemisinin
C. Diethylcarbamazine
D. Nifurtimox
E. Pyrimethamine

    • Explanation

    Although fever and fatigue are nonspecific symptoms, this patient's peripheral blood smear is diagnostic of malaria, a disease caused by 4 main species of Plasmodium. The most common species is Plasmodium falciparum, which is endemic to sub-Saharan Africa, New Guinea, the Dominican Republic, and Haiti. The second most prevalent species is Plasmodium vivax, which is endemic to Central and South America and the western Pacific region. The 2 other species (Plasmodium ovale, Plasmodium malariae) are responsible for only a small minority of cases.

    Malaria is most often diagnosed by examination of a peripheral blood smear with Giemsa staining. Because each of the 4 main species appears differently on blood smear, this test also helps identify the infecting species. In P falciparum infection, red blood cells have multiple small rings representing developing trophozoites (arrows) and banana-shaped gametocytes (not shown).

    For malaria acquired in areas known to have chloroquine-sensitive organisms, chloroquine is the treatment of choice. However, most African Plasmodium species are chloroquine resistant and require treatment with atovaquone-proguanil or artemisinins, which are well tolerated and have excellent cure rates. Because P vivax and P ovale have dormant liver forms (hypnozoites), primaquine therapy should be added to the treatment regimen for these species.

    (Choice A) Amphotericin B is used to treat leishmaniasis, a parasitic disease transmitted by sandflies. It is endemic to Africa, Southeast Asia, and the Americas. Visceral disease is typically marked by subacute fever, malaise, abdominal pain, and splenomegaly. Giemsa stain usually shows spherical/ovoid bodies (amastigotes) and rod-shaped inclusions (kinetoplast) in the skin/viscera.

    (Choice C) Diethylcarbamazine treats filariasis, a nematode infection of the lymphatics and subcutaneous tissue transmitted by mosquito vectors. Giemsa stain of a thick smear of blood would show microfilariae.

    (Choice D) Nifurtimox is used to treat trypanosomiasis, a protozoal illness endemic to Africa that causes sleeping sickness. CNS manifestations (eg, altered personality, stupor, excessive daytime somnolence) are prominent due to meningoencephalitis. Giemsa stain would show trypanosomes.

    (Choice E) Because most species of Plasmodium are now resistant to pyrimethamine, it is not typically used in treatment of malaria. Pyrimethamine can be used to treat toxoplasmosis, which most often causes symptomatic disease (eg, encephalitis) in patients with AIDS.

    Educational objective:

    Blood microscopy findings that show red blood cells filled with multiple small rings suggest Plasmodium infection (malaria). Erythrocytic forms of this parasite are treated with antimalarials such as chloroquine (in nonresistant areas), atovaquone-proguanil, or artemisinins. Primaquine is required to kill Plasmodium vivax and Plasmodium ovale liver hypnozoites.
