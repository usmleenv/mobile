A 52-year-old man is in the hospital burn unit 3 days after sustaining severe burns to 35% of his body surface area in a house fire. The patient remains intubated due to inhalational injury and has been undergoing staged excision and grafting of his burns. There has been no clinical evidence of infection, but the patient has been persistently febrile, hypertensive, tachycardiac, and hyperglycemic. Treatment with oxandrolone, a synthetic testosterone analogue, is considered. The beneficial effect of this treatment is most likely related to its effect on which of the following processes?

A. Cardiac sympathetic activity
B. Hepatic glucose production
C. Muscle protein catabolism
D. Sensory perception of pain
E. Vascular capillary permeability

    This patient with severe burns to 35% of his total body surface area (TBSA) has signs of a hypermetabolic response, including increased basal body temperature (eg, fever without clinical signs of infection), a hyperdynamic circulatory response (eg, hypertension, tachycardia), and persistent hyperglycemia. The hypermetabolic response typically arises within the first 5 days following burn injury and is likely triggered by the profound release of inflammatory mediators from damaged tissue, which leads to increased levels of catecholamines and glucocorticoids. The severity of the response increases proportionally with burn size.

    The increased basal metabolic rate of the hypermetabolic response leads to increased protein and lipid catabolism, often resulting in lean muscle wasting. To reduce loss of lean muscle mass, early nutritional support (eg, tube feeds) and anabolic steroid therapy (eg, oxandrolone) can be initiated. Oxandrolone is a synthetic testosterone analogue that enhances protein synthesis and decreases protein catabolism in skeletal myocytes. In patients with burn injury, it has been shown to both reduce lean muscle loss and decrease length of hospital stay.

    (Choice A) Increased circulating catecholamines after burn injury lead to increased cardiac sympathetic activity, typically manifesting as hypertension and tachycardia. Beta blockade (eg, propranolol) is commonly used to blunt these effects.

    (Choice B) Increased hepatic glucose production occurs as part of the hypermetabolic response to burn injury and contributes to hyperglycemia, which increases risk of infectious complications and mortality. Insulin is commonly required to provide glycemic control.

    (Choice D) Burn injuries cause intense pain, which can further increase sympathetic stimulation and metabolic rate. Opioid analgesia is commonly used to decrease sensory perception of pain in affected patients.

    (Choice E) Widespread tissue injury in large TBSA burns commonly leads to a profound systemic inflammatory response with increased vascular capillary permeability, especially within the first 24 hours. Aggressive fluid resuscitation is required during this period to prevent intravascular volume depletion (ie, burn shock).

    Educational objective:

    Patients with severe burn injury commonly develop a hypermetabolic response that results in an increased basal metabolic rate and, often, lean muscle wasting. Administration of oxandrolone, a synthetic testosterone analogue, enhances muscle protein synthesis and decreases protein catabolism, reducing lean muscle loss.
