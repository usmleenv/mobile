    • Explanation

    Fetal circulation is characterized by:

    • High pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR): In utero, the lungs are filled with amniotic fluid, which causes diffuse vasoconstriction of the pulmonary capillary beds (hypoxic vasoconstriction).
    • Low systemic vascular resistance (SVR): The fetal systemic circulation is connected in parallel to the low-resistance placental vascular bed via the umbilical arteries (which deliver deoxygenated blood to the placenta) and umbilical vein (which delivers oxygenated blood to the fetus).

    In the fetus, PVR is greater than SVR; the foramen ovale and ductus arteriosus allow blood to be shunted away from the high resistance pulmonary circulation into the low resistance systemic circulation (ie, right-to-left shunting), bypassing the lungs. Because the ductus arteriosus is a high-caliber shunt, the right ventricle contributes approximately 60 percent of the fetal systemic blood flow (and the left ventricle contributes the remainder).

    At birth, fetal circulation transitions to neonatal circulation, which is characterized by:

    • Decreased PVR: Pulmonary expansion and aeration lead to diffuse pulmonary vasodilation.
    • Increased SVR: Umbilical cord clamping abruptly removes the low-resistance placenta from neonatal circulation.

    Because SVR is now greater than PVR, shunting through the ductus arteriosus reverses to left-to-right shunting (triggering eventual closure of the ductus). With the cessation of right-to-left shunting through the ductus arteriosus at birth, only the left ventricle contributes to systemic blood flow.

    Educational objective:

    Before birth, high pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR) and low systemic vascular resistance (SVR) facilitate right-to-left shunting through the ductus arteriosus, and both ventricles contribute to fetal systemic blood flow. At birth, decreased PVR (due to oxygenation and ventilation of the lungs) and increased SVR (due to umbilical cord clamping) reverse shunting through the ductus arteriosus (ie, left-to-right shunting), and only the left ventricle contributes to systemic blood flow.
