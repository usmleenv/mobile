    • Explanation

    This patient's widely split S2 that does not vary with the respiratory cycle (ie, fixed) and systolic ejection murmur at the left upper sternal border (ie, pulmonic flow murmur) are characteristic of an atrial septal defect (ASD). ASD creates left-to-right shunting of blood from the left atrium to the right atrium, increasing blood volume in the right atrium (ie, venous return from the vena cava plus blood delivered via the shunt) and causing right-sided volume overload. Over time, this leads to right atrial and right ventricular enlargement.

    Pulmonary arterial blood flow is also increased with ASD, but left-sided volume overload does not occur because additional blood volume returning to the left atrium escapes back to the right atrium without affecting the LV. Most patients with ASD remain asymptomatic until adolescence or adulthood but can eventually have signs and symptoms of right-sided heart failure (eg, dyspnea, fatigue, parasternal heave). Severe pulmonary hypertension and Eisenmenger syndrome can also sometimes develop with ASD left unrepaired for many years.

    In contrast to ASD, a left-to-right shunt distal to the atrioventricular valves (ie, ventricular septal defect, patent ductus arteriosus) can cause prominent pulmonary overcirculation that, due to the absence of ASD, must be accomodated by the left atrium and LV; as such, these shunts initially present with left-sided (rather than right-sided) volume overload. In addition, pulmonary pressures are often higher with VSD and PDA because these defects expose the pulmonary circulation to higher systemic-level arterial pressure. As such, they frequently cause symptoms much earlier in life compared to ASD, and the risk for severe pulmonary hypertension and Eisenmenger syndrome is greater than with ASD.

    Educational objective:

    Atrial septal defects cause left-to-right atrial shunting and right-sided volume overload with enlargement of the right atrium and right ventricle; left-sided volume overload does not occur as excess flow entering the left atrium cycles back into the right atrium through the septal defect. In contrast, left-to-right shunts distal to the atrioventricular valves (eg, ventricular septal defect, patent ductus arteriosus) initially cause left-sided volume overload.
