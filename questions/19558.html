A 14-year-old girl is brought to the office to establish medical care. Vital signs are within normal limits. Cardiac examination is significant for a murmur that is best heard at the left upper sternal border. Phonocardiogram obtained at this location reveals the findings shown below:

This patient's murmur is most likely due to which of the following mechanisms?

A. Abnormal narrowing of the aortic valve
B. Incomplete ventricular septum development
C. Increased blood flow to the lungs
D. Regurgitation of blood from the aorta

    • Explanation

    This patient's phonocardiogram shows an early-peaking systolic murmur as well as a widely split S2 that is fixed (ie, no variation between inspiration and expiration). These findings are characteristic of an atrial septal defect (ASD), a common congenital heart lesion resulting in an abnormal connection between the right and left atria.

    Left atrial pressure is normally slightly higher than in the right atrium due to the greater compliance of the thin-walled right ventricle; as a result, ASDs cause left-to-right shunting. This blood flow has low velocity and turbulence and therefore does not create a murmur directly. Instead, the increased volume of blood delivered to the right heart (ie, normal venous return plus extra blood through the shunt) results in increased blood flow into the pulmonary arteries across the pulmonic valve. This creates the characteristic early-peaking midsystolic ejection murmur over the left upper sternal border (ie, pulmonic flow murmur), as seen in this patient. Increased blood flow through the tricuspid valve may also cause a diastolic rumble at the left lower sternal border (not present in this patient).

    Increased blood flow across the pulmonic valve also accounts for the characteristic ASD finding of a widely split, fixed S2. Under normal conditions, splitting of S2 is heard only during inspiration when there is increased venous return to the right heart. This results in delayed pulmonic valve closure (P2) relative to aortic valve closure (A2) due to the increased time needed for right ventricular blood ejection. With ASD, right ventricular volume remains increased throughout the entire respiratory cycle, leading to a persistently delayed P2 with no apparent respiratory variation in splitting.

    (Choice A) Aortic stenosis causes a crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur over the right second intercostal space. A2 may be delayed until after P2 (paradoxical splitting) due to the increase in left ventricular ejection time.

    (Choice B) Ventricular septal defects create a holosystolic murmur along the left lower sternal border due to a high-pressure, left-to-right shunt. Splitting of S2 may be widened but is usually not fixed. Fixed splitting is more typical of ASDs because atrial shunts reduce the normal variation in right ventricular volume during the respiratory cycle (flow through the ASD increases when venous return decreases, minimizing inspiratory/expiratory differences in right ventricular volume).

    (Choice D) Aortic insufficiency is characterized by regurgitant blood flow through the aortic valve, resulting in a decrescendo diastolic murmur along the sternal border. Splitting of S2 is usually not affected.

    Educational objective:

    An atrial septal defect leads to increased right-sided blood flow due to left-to-right shunting. Characteristic findings are an early-peaking midsystolic ejection murmur over the left upper sternal border (ie, pulmonic flow murmur) and wide and fixed splitting of S2.
