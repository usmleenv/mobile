    [E] = excitatory (postsynaptic potential); [I] = inhibitory postsynaptic potential; cAMP = cyclic adenosine monophosphate; PKA = protein kinase A.

    The top panel in this experiment demonstrates a classic excitatory postsynaptic potential (EPSP), a small depolarization that moves the postsynaptic neuron closer to the threshold for full action potential firing. The EPSP occurs with rising intracellular calcium (bottom panel), indicating that the involved postsynaptic receptor acts, at least in part, by increasing calcium conductance.

    The glutamate NMDA receptor becomes permeable to cations causing net depolarization (sodium and calcium in > potassium out) when bound by glutamate. The increased calcium entry also initiates second-messenger signaling cascades (eg, protein kinase C) to regulate synaptic plasticity; repetitive excitation of postsynaptic neurons renders them more responsive, producing stronger EPSPs over time. This process, known as long-term potentiation (LTP), dictates synaptic strength between hippocampal neurons and plays a critical role in the molecular basis of memory, learning, and addiction.

    Because of its role in memory, acute blockade of glutamate NMDA receptors confers the amnestic property of certain medications (eg, ketamine) and some drugs of illicit use (eg, phencyclidine [PCP]). However, chronic blockade (eg, memantine) can exert neuroprotective effects by dampening glutamate-induced excitotoxicity (ie, neuron apoptosis due to calcium overload).

    (Choice A) Dopamine can be either excitatory or inhibitory depending on the receptor subtype. D2 receptors are generally coupled to Gi proteins that inhibit protein kinase A, leading to the opening of potassium channels that inhibit neuronal depolarization.

    (Choice B) GABA is the chief inhibitory neurotransmitter, causing postsynaptic hyperpolarization via increased chloride and bicarbonate influx (ie, ionotropic GABA-A ion channel) or increased potassium efflux (ie, metabotropic GABA-B receptor).

    (Choice D) The glycine receptor causes inhibitory postsynaptic hyperpolarization via increasing membrane permeability to chloride. This receptor mediates inhibitory interneuron transmission in the spinal cord.

    (Choice E) The norepinephrine alpha2 receptor is located predominantly on presynaptic (not postsynaptic) nerve terminals throughout the CNS and the peripheral nervous system. Activation of these receptors inhibits norepinephrine release from the nerve terminal, inducing sympatholytic effects (eg, reduced heart rate, vasodilation).

    Educational objective:

    Glutamate is the main excitatory neurotransmitter of the CNS, inducing postsynaptic neuron depolarization and calcium entry through the NMDA receptor. Calcium entry programs the neuron to depolarize more robustly to repeated stimulation; this long-term potentiation forms a molecular basis for memory, learning, and addiction.
