A 46-year-old man is brought to the emergency department due to worsening blurry vision, difficulty swallowing, and dry mouth for the past hour. The patient has also been having nausea and abdominal discomfort but no headache or fever. He has no prior medical conditions and takes no medications. The patient has not traveled recently, but he attended a community potluck event yesterday where he sampled home-canned foods. Physical examination shows bilateral ptosis and sluggish pupillary response. Which of the following steps in neuromuscular transmission is most likely affected in this patient?

    • Explanation

    This patient with gastrointestinal discomfort has manifestations of both nicotinic blockade (eg, dysphagia, ptosis) and muscarinic blockade (eg, dry mouth) that developed after he ate home-canned food. This is strongly suggestive of food poisoning with Clostridium botulinum toxin, a preformed neurotoxin. Botulism characteristically presents as a symmetric descending paralysis that first manifests with cranial nerve abnormalities (diplopia, dysphagia, dysphonia [3Ds]). Nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain may also occur in foodborne botulism, which classically occurs 12-36 hours after consumption of contaminated foods that are fermented, preserved, or canned improperly at home.

    Botulinum toxin causes disease by inhibiting nicotinic and muscarinic motor neurons. It enters the nerve terminals through endocytosis, where it prevents binding and fusion of acetylcholine-containing synaptic vesicles with the plasma membrane through the destruction of SNARE proteins. This effectively blocks ACh release into the synapse. Botulinum toxin is highly potent; a very small quantity can be lethal without ventilatory support. The toxin is heat labile, so disease can be prevented by thoroughly heating food prior to consumption.

    (Choices A, B, and C) The synthesis of acetylcholine in the neuron requires uptake of choline into the cell; then choline acetyltransferase synthesizes ACh from acetate and choline. ACh is subsequently transported into secretory vesicles in preparation for secretion. These steps are not impacted by botulinum toxin.

    (Choice E) Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors such as organophosphates, which are commonly used as insecticides, prevent ACh breakdown. Manifestations of organophosphate poisoning reflect depolarizing blockade (eg, muscle weakness, fasciculations, paralysis), CNS effects (eg, lethargy, seizures), and muscarinic overstimulation (eg, miosis, bradycardia, increased lacrimation and salivation).

    (Choice F) Anticholinergic poisoning due to competitive antagonists of muscarinic ACh receptors (eg, atropine, scopolamine) is relatively common and can manifest as cutaneous vasodilation, anhidrosis, hyperthermia, mydriasis, and delirium. However, these agents have no effect on the nicotinic ACh receptors controlling voluntary muscles. Poisoning due to nicotinic receptor antagonists (eg, pancuronium, tubocurarine) is uncommon because these agents have poor oral absorption.

    Educational objective:

    Botulinum toxin blocks the presynaptic exocytosis of ACh vesicles, causing impaired muscarinic and nicotinic neurotransmission. It characteristically presents as a symmetric descending paralysis that manifests with cranial nerve abnormalities, often following consumption of home-canned foods.
