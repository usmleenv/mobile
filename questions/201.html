A 20-year-old man comes to the office due to occasional palpitations that he describes as skipped beats. He has no chest pain or shortness of breath. Medical history is unremarkable. On physical examination, the patient has wide, fixed splitting of S2. No murmurs are heard. Chest x-ray reveals enlargement of the right heart contour. Echocardiographic findings are consistent with a congenital heart defect. Repair of this patient's congenital disease is aimed primarily at preventing irreversible changes to which of the following?

A. Coronary vessels
B. Left atrium
C. Left ventricle
D. Pulmonary vessels
E. Right atrium

    • Explanation

    This patient has wide, fixed splitting of S2, a characteristic auscultatory finding of an atrial septal defect (ASD). ASD permits left-to-right shunting due to higher pressure in the left atrium compared to the right. The increase in right atrial blood flow causes right-sided volume overload, leading to delayed closure of the pulmonic valve (wide S2) that does not vary with respirations (fixed S2). The low-velocity blood flow through the ASD does not typically produce a murmur; the characteristic murmur of ASD, present in some patients, is a midsystolic ejection murmur resulting from increased flow across the pulmonic valve.

    Right-sided volume overload eventually leads to right atrial and ventricular enlargement, which may be seen as an enlarged right heart contour on chest x-ray. Atrial fibrillation, which is the likely cause of this patient's palpitations, is a potential complication resulting from chronic right atrial stretching.

    With long-standing ASD, the chronic increase in blood flow through the pulmonary circulation induces laminated medial hypertrophy of the pulmonary arteries. This increases pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR), causing pulmonary hypertension. Eventually, PVR can exceed systemic vascular resistance, reversing the direction of the intracardiac shunt (Eisenmenger syndrome); this allows blood to bypass the pulmonary circulation and can cause adult-onset cyanosis, clubbing, and polycythemia.

    Pulmonary vascular remodeling is irreversible. Therefore, early closure of a hemodynamically significant ASD is needed to avoid irreversible pulmonary vascular changes.

    (Choices A, B, and C) ASD does not typically cause volume overload or enlargement of the left heart chambers because excess blood flow is shunted to the right atrium before reaching the left ventricle. The coronary vessels are not typically affected.

    (Choice E) ASD causes right atrial dilation, but the consequence of this change is far less than that of the pulmonary vascular changes. In addition, right atrial dilation is mostly reversible because, unlike in the pulmonary arteries and often the right ventricle, significant muscular hypertrophy does not take place.

    Educational objective:

    Atrial septal defects cause wide, fixed splitting of S2 due to right-sided volume overload from left-to-right shunting. Uncorrected defects can lead to irreversible medial hypertrophy of the pulmonary arteries with pulmonary hypertension and reversal to right-to-left shunting (ie, Eisenmenger syndrome).
