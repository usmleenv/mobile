    This patient has infectious urethritis and epididymitis due to Chlamydia trachomatis. C trachomatis urethral infections are often asymptomatic but patients may develop dysuria and scant, watery discharge; deeper spread to the epididymis is typically marked by scrotal pain with tenderness along the posterior aspect of the testis.

    Laboratory results in patients with infectious urethritis due to C trachomatis are as follows:

    • Pyuria: Urine dipstick testing will show positive leukocyte esterase, and urine Gram stain will reveal ‚â•10 white blood cells/hpf (Choice E).
    • Poor Gram staining: Gram stain of urethral swab can quickly diagnose Neisseria gonorrhoeae infection (intracellular gram-negative diplococci); however, organisms will not be seen in the setting of C trachomatis or Mycoplasma genitalium infection because these pathogens do not have sufficient peptidoglycan in their cell wall to pick up Gram stain.
    • Negative urine culture: Because C trachomatis is an obligate intracellular pathogen, it cannot be grown on traditional culture medium; therefore, urine and genital swab cultures will be negative.

    The microbial diagnosis of C trachomatis infection is generally established with urine (or cervical) nucleic acid amplification testing.

    (Choice A) Positive urine white blood cell, Gram stain, and urine culture results are typical of a urinary tract infection caused by Escherichia coli or Klebsiella pneumoniae. However, obligate intracellular bacteria such as C trachomatis are not seen on Gram stain or easily cultured.

    (Choice B) Patients with N gonorrhoeae infection usually have pyuria, but have a negative urine culture because a specialized medium (eg, Thayer-Martin agar) is typically required to grow the organism. Gram stain of urethral discharge would show gram-negative intracellular diplococci.

    (Choice D) Positive urine Gram stain and culture with no pyuria may be seen in some patients with asymptomatic bacteriuria, a typically benign condition except in select situations (eg, pregnancy).

    Educational objective:

    Urinalysis in patients with Chlamydia trachomatis infection classically shows sterile pyuria (positive urine white blood cells, no bacteria on Gram stain, no growth on culture) because the C trachomatis bacterium is an obligate intracellular bacterium with minimal peptidoglycan in its cell wall. The diagnostic test of choice is nucleic acid amplification testing.
