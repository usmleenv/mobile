A 45-year-old man participates in a research study. He undergoes percutaneous skeletal muscle biopsy. Microscopic analysis shows his type I and type II muscle fibers are clustered in groups in contrast to a normal intermixed pattern as shown below.

Which of the following is the most likely cause of the changes observed in this patient's skeletal muscle?

A. Autoimmune muscle injury
B. Dystrophin protein mutation
C. Impaired glycogen metabolism
D. Motor axon loss and reinnervation
E. Statin-induced muscle damage

    • Explanation

    This patient's biopsy demonstrates muscle fiber-type grouping, in which the normal checkerboard pattern of type I and type II muscle fibers is lost, and fibers of the same type are grouped together. This is characteristic of muscle reinnervation after denervation. Skeletal muscle fibers are categorized into 2 major types:

    • Type I fibers (slow-twitch) specialize in stamina by using oxidative phosphorylation in the mitochondria for steady and sustained ATP production. The high content of myoglobin gives these fibers a red color.
    • Type II fibers (fast-twitch) specialize in rapid bursts of strength by using anaerobic glycolysis. The lower quantities of mitochondria and myoglobin lead to a pale color.

    Muscle fibers are arranged into a motor unit composed of the same fiber type, with each unit innervated by a single motor neuron. On histologic section, these motor units form a characteristic mixed checkerboard pattern of type I and type II fibers.

    When muscle denervation first takes place, there is atrophy of both types of muscle fibers. The affected myocytes then stimulate nearby nerves to undergo collateral sprouting to reinnervate the muscle fibers, which usually results in adjacent muscle fibers being innervated by the same neuron. Because the muscle fiber type is dependent on the characteristics of the innervating neuron, reinnervation typically leads to clustering of muscle fiber types.

    (Choice A) Autoimmune muscle injury (eg, dermatomyositis, polymyositis) often shows an inflammatory cellular infiltrate with myofiber necrosis, fragmentation, and phagocytosis. Over time, the active inflammation and necrosis are replaced by fibrosis.

    (Choice B) Duchenne and Becker muscular dystrophies are caused by mutations affecting dystrophin, a cytoplasmic protein vital for stabilization of the muscle cell membrane. Loss of cell membrane integrity leads to muscle fiber degeneration and fibrofatty replacement.

    (Choice C) Certain types of glycogen storage diseases can lead to weakness and fatigue due to abnormal glycogenolysis in skeletal muscle. Muscle biopsy would show enlarged lysosomes containing periodic acid-Schiff (PAS)-positive material, but no change in the arrangement of muscle fiber types.

    (Choice E) Statin-induced muscle damage is often characterized by myofiber necrosis without an associated inflammatory infiltrate. Muscle aches and weakness are the most common complications of statin therapy and are likely due, at least in part, to decreased production of coenzyme Q10.

    Educational objective:

    With muscle denervation and reinnervation, nerve fiber collateral sprouting results in adjacent muscle fibers being innervated by the same neuron. This leads to loss of the normal histologic checkerboard pattern of type I and type II muscle fibers and results in muscle fiber-type grouping.
