A 34-year-old woman is being evaluated for fatigue and progressive exertional dyspnea. She goes mountain biking with friends a few times a month and recently noticed that she has to stop and rest more frequently when traveling uphill. The patient has no significant medical history and takes no medications. She occasionally drinks alcohol but does not use tobacco or recreational drugs. Her father died of a myocardial infarction at age 72. Lungs are clear on auscultation. An echocardiogram reveals an enlarged coronary sinus. Which of the following is the most likely cause of this finding?

A. Aortic dissection
B. Coronary artery disease
C. Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
D. Pericardial effusion
E. Pulmonary hypertension

    • Explanation

    This patient with progressive exertional dyspnea and coronary sinus dilation most likely has idiopathic pulmonary arterial hypertension, a rare condition that most commonly affects relatively young women.

    The coronary sinus is the largest cardiac vein and is located in the left atrioventricular groove on the posterior aspect of the heart. It collects most of the venous drainage from the myocardium and delivers it to the right atrium through an orifice adjacent to the entrance of the inferior vena cava.

    Because the coronary sinus communicates directly with the right atrium, it is affected by pressure changes within the right atrium. Coronary sinus dilation seen on echocardiography is usually an indication of elevated right-sided heart pressure, as occurs with pulmonary hypertension. Less commonly, dilation can result from markedly increased blood flow through the coronary sinus, which can occur with some congenital heart defects (eg, total anomalous pulmonary venous return).

    (Choice A) Aortic dissection most commonly occurs in patients with systemic hypertension. Elevated right-sided heart pressure with coronary sinus dilation is not expected.

    (Choice B) Coronary artery disease (CAD) has no direct effect on the coronary sinus. Severe CAD can cause left ventricular systolic dysfunction and left-sided heart failure (ie, ischemic heart disease) that can eventually lead to pulmonary hypertension and right-sided heart failure. However, substantial CAD is unlikely in this relatively young woman.

    (Choice C) Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy usually involves asymmetric septal hypertrophy that often causes left ventricular outflow tract obstruction. Although left-sided heart pressures are commonly elevated, right-sided heart pressures are rarely substantially elevated.

    (Choice D) Pericardial effusion can cause extrinsic compression (not dilation) of the coronary sinus.

    Educational objective:

    The coronary sinus communicates freely with the right atrium and becomes dilated secondary to elevated right atrial pressure. The most common cause of coronary sinus dilation is pulmonary hypertension leading to elevated right-sided heart pressure.
