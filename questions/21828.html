    • Explanation

    This patient cannot reabsorb bicarbonate (HCO3‚àí) in the proximal convoluted tubule (PCT), which is characteristic of type 2 renal tubular acidosis (RTA). Type 2 RTA commonly occurs as part of a generalized resorptive dysfunction affecting proximal tubular cells (ie, Fanconi syndrome). Patients presenting in infancy or childhood often have an underlying genetic condition (eg, cystinosis, galactosemia, Dent disease) that predisposes to tubular dysfunction.

    The PCT is normally responsible for reabsorbing HCO3‚àí as well as glucose, phosphate, potassium, water, and amino acids. The proximal tubule damage that occurs in Fanconi syndrome leads to loss of these substances in the urine, producing the following clinical effects:

    • Inability to resorb HCO3‚àí results in a normal anion gap metabolic acidosis. The resulting decrease in serum HCO3‚àí is compensated by increased serum chloride (Cl‚àí) to maintain electronegative balance.
    • Excessive tubular glucose impairs renal concentrating ability by acting as an osmotic diuretic, contributing to development of polyuria (eg, more wet diapers) and episodes of hypovolemia . However, plasma glucose is tightly regulated by mechanisms (eg, insulin, glucagon) independent of renal reabsorption. Therefore, serum glucose remains normal.
    • Hypophosphatemia leads to rickets (eg, widening of wrist joints, frontal bossing, bowing of the legs).
    • Hypokalemia can cause muscle weakness.
    • Loss of amino acids and low-molecular-weight proteins does not typically have a significant effect, but aminoaciduria can be a general marker of proximal tubular dysfunction.

    Chronic acidosis, hypophosphatemia, and hypokalemia also contribute to growth failure, as seen in this patient.

    Educational objective:

    The proximal convoluted tubule (PCT) is responsible for reabsorption of bicarbonate as well as glucose, phosphate, potassium, water, and amino acids. Generalized PCT dysfunction (eg, Fanconi syndrome) results in urinary loss of these substances; in addition, serum chloride rises to maintain electronegative balance from bicarbonate loss and serum glucose remains normal due to intact counterregulatory mechanisms.
