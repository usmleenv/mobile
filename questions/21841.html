A 92-year-old woman is being evaluated due to the presence of skin blisters for the past week. The patient is bed-bound and fatigued. Vital signs are within normal limits. She has no blisters or erosions in the mouth. Skin findings are shown in the image below:

Serologic studies are most likely to show an autoantibody directed against which of the following components?

A. Connexins
B. Desmosomal proteins
C. Hemidesmosal proteins
D. Integrins
E. Intermediate filaments

    • Explanation

    This elderly patient with new-onset bullae likely has bullous pemphigoid (BP), an autoimmune blistering disease. BP most commonly affects individuals age >60. As in this patient, bullae are typically tense and have erythematous or normal-appearing bases. Lesion distribution can be localized or diffuse, involving the trunk and extremities; mucosal involvement is rare. The pruritus preceding and associated with this condition can be severe.

    BP is caused by autoantibodies against hemidesmosomes, structural components which anchor the basal layer of epithelial cells to the basement membrane. These autoantibodies result in a destructive inflammatory cascade that causes the entire epidermis to separate from the dermis, forming subepidermal blisters. In addition to skin biopsy for routine histology and immunofluorescence, serologic testing for autoantibodies against hemidesmosome proteins (eg, BP180, BP230) is also obtained to confirm the diagnosis.

    (Choice A) Connexins are proteins that form gap junctions, which allow small molecules and ions (eg, cyclic AMP, Na+) to pass between cells so that they may function in a coordinated manner (eg, contraction of cardiac myocytes).

    (Choice B) Autoantibodies directed at desmosome proteins (eg, desmogleins 1 and 3) cause pemphigus vulgaris, which presents with flaccid bullae that easily rupture and form erosions. Mucosal involvement is almost always present.

    (Choice D) Integrins expressed on the cell surface of leukocytes are responsible for diapedesis from the circulation into tissue.

    (Choice E) Epidermolysis bullosa simplex is a mucocutaneous blistering disorder caused by genetic mutations of (not autoantibodies against) the proteins forming intermediate filaments (the major intracellular structural component of keratinocytes). This condition typically presents early in life.

    Educational objective:

    Bullous pemphigoid (BP) is an autoimmune blistering disorder characterized by tense bullae in patients age >60. Autoantibodies against hemidesmosome components initiate an inflammatory response that results in separation of the epidermis from the dermis, leading to subepidermal blisters.
