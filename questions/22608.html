A 59-year-old man comes to the office for evaluation of a sore on his ear. The lesion has been present for the past 4 months and has shown little change. Physical examination reveals a 1-cm firm, scaly, pink nodule over the right pinna. Biopsy of the lesion shows numerous dysplastic eosinophilic keratinocytes spanning the full thickness of the epidermis and dermis. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Actinic keratosis
B. Basal cell carcinoma
C. Seborrheic keratosis
D. Squamous cell carcinoma
E. Verruca vulgaris

    This patient's persistent skin lesion in a sun-exposed area (eg, ear) is concerning for skin cancer, particularly basal cell carcinoma (BCC) or cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (SCC). Both BCC and cutaneous SCC, which are composed of malignant keratinocytes, can present as a skin-colored nodule. Sometimes differences in gross appearance and texture can help distinguish between the two neoplasms:

    • Nodular BCC classically has a translucent or pearly appearance, often with central telangiectasias.
    • Cutaneous SCC classically has a rough or scaly (keratinized) surface.

    BCC and SCC are not always distinguishable based on gross findings (eg, either may develop ulceration and/or crusting). Therefore, biopsy is essential to make the correct diagnosis:

    • BCC: Biopsy typically shows nests of basaloid cells (ie, resembling the stratum basale) that appear dark blue due to their basophilic, hyperchromatic nuclei and scant cytoplasm. Cells at the periphery frequently display parallel alignment (ie, peripheral palisading) (Choice B).
    • SCC: Biopsy typically shows nests of dysplastic keratinocytes, which often have abundant cytoplasm that appears eosinophilic due to keratin filaments. Keratin pearls (also eosinophilic) are commonly present.

    Dermal involvement represents invasion of the dysplastic keratinocytes past the basement membrane, classifying this patient's lesion as invasive (rather than in situ) SCC.

    (Choice A) Actinic keratoses also consist of dysplastic keratinocytes and can appear as scaly lesions on sun-exposed areas. However, they are premalignant (vs malignant) lesions; the dysplastic keratinocytes do not involve the full thickness of the epidermis (eg, SCC in situ) or invade the dermis (eg, invasive SCC).

    (Choice C) Seborrheic keratoses usually have a velvety or greasy (vs scaly) surface and a "stuck-on" appearance. Because they are a result of benign clonal expansion of immature keratinocytes, histologic examination typically reveals proliferation of small basaloid cells that are not dysplastic. Hyperkeratosis (thickened stratum corneum) and keratin-containing cysts are commonly seen.

    (Choice E) Verruca vulgaris (ie, common wart) due to human papillomavirus often presents as a nodule or plaque with a rough, hyperkeratotic surface. However, histologic examination typically reveals acanthosis (thickened epidermis), hyperkeratosis, and koilocytosis (keratinocytes with cytoplasmic vacuolization); dysplastic keratinocytes invading the dermis are not seen.

    Educational objective:

    Cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) classically presents as a rough or scaly (keratinized) nodule. Diagnosis is made with biopsy, which typically reveals nests of dysplastic keratinocytes with eosinophilic cytoplasm (due to keratin filaments). Dermal involvement signifies invasive SCC.
