    This patient's clinical presentation is consistent with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM). Many patients with HCM are asymptomatic and are recognized when an abnormal murmur or ECG is noted during evaluation for other purposes. HCM typically involves left ventricular (LV) enlargement that is most prominent in the basal anterior septum and can lead to LV outflow tract obstruction. The clinical manifestations, which depend on the extent of hypertrophy and outflow tract obstruction, include:

    • exertional dyspnea, chest pain, fatigue, palpitations, presyncope, or syncope. Sudden cardiac arrest can also occur.
    • a harsh crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur best heard at the apex and left sternal border, with characteristic changes in intensity during physiologic maneuvers.

    The condition is inherited in autosomal dominant fashion, resulting from mutations in genes encoding for myocardial contractile proteins of the sarcomere. The proteins cardiac myosin binding protein C and cardiac beta-myosin heavy chain are most commonly affected. The phenotypic manifestations of the disease (eg, septal enlargement) typically begin to develop around the time of adolescence.

    Despite the autosomal dominant inheritance, affected patients may not always have an identifiable first-degree relative who is similarly affected because not all mutations responsible for HCM have been identified and many known mutations have variable penetrance.

    Educational objective:

    Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) is an autosomal dominant genetic disorder caused by mutation in one of several genes encoding the myocardial contractile proteins of the cardiac sarcomere. Mutations that affect cardiac myosin binding protein C or cardiac beta-myosin heavy chain are responsible for the majority of identifiable mutations in patients with HCM.
