    This patient's constitutional symptoms (eg, malaise, fatigue), fever, and a new systolic murmur are highly suggestive of infective endocarditis (IE). IE is characterized by an infection of one or more heart valves, typically from bacteria (eg, Staphylococcus aureus). IE can involve multiple organs (eg, brain, lungs, skin, kidneys) via immunologic and vascular phenomena. Renal involvement is usually due to one of the following:

    • Circulating immune complex‚Äìmediated injury (immunologic phenomena). Circulating bacterial antigens and antibodies form small immune complexes (ie, type III hypersensitivity) that deposit within the glomerular capillary wall, causing glomerulonephritis.
    • Thromboembolic disease (vascular phenomena): Emboli from infected endocardial vegetations may metastasize via the hematogenous route to cause renal infarction.

    This patient has hematuria with red blood cell casts and acute kidney injury (AKI) consistent with glomerulonephritis (ie, nephritic syndrome). Therefore, circulating immune complex‚Äìmediated injury is likely; renal biopsy typically shows capillary wall thickening with subendothelial and subepithelial deposit formation. Other causes of immune complex‚Äìmediated renal injury include postinfectious glomerulonephritis, systemic lupus erythematosus, and mixed cryoglobulinemia‚Äîall of which are unlikely in this patient with evidence of a current bacterial infection.

    (Choice A) Antiglomerular basement membrane (anti-GBM) antibodies can also cause glomerulonephritis. However, these antibodies do not affect cardiac tissues and would not cause a new cardiac murmur. Anti-GBM antibodies may target the pulmonary alveolar basement membrane, causing hemoptysis (Goodpasture syndrome).

    (Choice C) IE is rarely caused by gram-negative organisms, which produce endotoxin. Although endotoxin production can contribute to AKI due to acute tubular necrosis (eg, muddy brown casts) in patients with gram-negative sepsis, glomerulonephritis would not be expected.

    (Choice D) Bacteremia in patients with IE can result in hematogenous spread to other organs, including the kidneys, resulting in abscess formation. However, urinalysis would likely show bacteria and associated pyuria (both absent in this patient).

    (Choice E) Although thromboembolism of vegetations can lead to renal infarctions, this is unlikely in this patient with AKI because a significant rise in serum creatinine would require a large amount of renal parenchyma to be affected. Moreover, renal infarction commonly causes flank or abdominal pain (absent in this patient).

    Educational objective:

    Infective endocarditis (eg, constitutional symptoms, fever, new cardiac murmur) can affect multiple organs, including the kidneys. Glomerulonephritis typically occurs due to injury caused by circulating immune complex‚Äìmediated injury (type III hypersensitivity), which is characterized by acute kidney injury and hematuria with red blood cell casts.
