A 54-year-old man is admitted to the hospital following a high-speed motor vehicle collision. He is found to have long bone fractures of the right leg that require open reduction/internal fixation. After the patient's condition is stabilized, he is transferred to a skilled nursing facility for extended rehabilitation. During his stay, his right leg is immobilized for several weeks. Management is complicated by decreased nutritional intake due to adverse effects of opioid pain medications. Measurement of the thigh following removal of casting materials finds a 2-cm decrease in diameter. The underlying process responsible for this patient's change in body measurement is most likely to also occur in which of the following?

A. Adrenal glands during prolonged glucocorticoid therapy
B. Bronchial tissue after prolonged tobacco smoke exposure
C. Endometrial tissue during estrogen replacement therapy
D. Esophageal mucosa on repeated acid exposure
E. Heart muscle during long-standing hypertension

    This patient experienced a decreased physical workload when his leg was immobilized. As a result, the skeletal muscle fibers in the leg decreased in size, leading to disuse atrophy.

    Atrophy is tissue loss due to reduced cell size and number (ie, cellular death). It may be physiologic (eg, wolffian duct atrophy in the female fetus, maternal uterus atrophy following childbirth) or pathologic. Pathologic atrophy has a variety of causes, including lack of use, loss of hormonal or neurologic stimulation (denervation), impaired circulation, mechanical compression, aging, and nutritional insufficiency. On a cellular level, it represents an attempt to decrease the cell's metabolic requirements via a reduction in organelles (eg, mitochondria, rough endoplasmic reticulum) and protein synthesis, as well as an increase in protein breakdown. In prolonged atrophic states, irreversibly damaged cells may undergo apoptosis, leading to reduced cellular numbers.

    Pathologic atrophy also occurs in the adrenal cortex during prolonged glucocorticoid use due to suppression of ACTH release. For this reason, abrupt cessation of exogenous glucocorticoid therapy can lead to acute adrenal insufficiency and adrenal crisis.

    (Choice B) Prolonged exposure to tobacco smoke can induce metaplasia in the bronchial epithelium. This results in the normal columnar epithelium being replaced by squamous epithelium (ie, squamous metaplasia), causing an associated risk of squamous cell carcinoma.

    (Choice C) After menopause, falling estrogen levels can lead to atrophy of the breasts, endometrium, and vaginal mucosa. Conversely, estrogen replacement in these patients can cause endometrial hyperplasia (ie, increased cell number) unless combined with progesterone therapy.

    (Choice D) In chronic gastroesophageal reflux, esophageal mucosa subjected to repeated acid exposure may undergo metaplasia, in which the normal squamous epithelium is replaced by columnar epithelium with goblet cells (ie, Barrett esophagus).

    (Choice E) Heart muscle subjected to long-standing hypertension is prone to developing hypertrophy (ie, increase in cell size), not atrophy.

    Educational objective:

    Atrophy is a cellular adaptation characterized by a decrease in cell size and number, leading to decreased organ size. Pathologic atrophy has a variety of causes, including decreased physical workload, loss of innervation, decreased blood supply, inadequate nutrition, absent endocrine stimulation, aging, and mechanical pressure.
