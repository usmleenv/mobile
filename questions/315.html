An 8-day-old boy is being evaluated in the neonatal intensive care unit. The patient was born at 27 weeks gestation and intubated at birth due to surfactant deficiency. Temperature is 37 C (98.6 F), blood pressure is 66/12 mm Hg, and pulse is 182/min. A continuous harsh murmur is heard at the left infraclavicular region. There are bounding femoral and palmar pulses. A drug with which of the following mechanisms of action is most appropriate for treating the cause of this patient's murmur?

A. Activation of phospholipase A2
B. Activation of prostacyclin receptors
C. Activation of prostaglandin E2 receptors
D. Inhibition of cyclooxygenase
E. Inhibition of lipoxygenase
F. Inhibition of prostaglandin D2 receptors

    • Explanation

    This newborn has a continuous murmur, widened pulse pressure, and bounding pulses, findings characteristic of a patent ductus arteriosus (PDA). After birth, closure of the ductus arteriosus normally occurs within 24-48 hours and is initiated by loss of placental prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) and the vasoconstrictive effects of increased systemic oxygen levels.

    Persistence of the ductus arteriosus (ie, a PDA) most commonly occurs in premature infants and may be associated with elevated postnatal PGE2 or increased sensitivity to prostaglandins. Left-to-right shunting from the aorta to the pulmonary artery takes place, causing a continuous machinery-like murmur. Patients with a large PDA can develop decreased diastolic blood pressure (due to reduced systemic blood flow) and pulmonary overcirculation with left ventricular volume overload, leading to a compensatory increase in stroke volume that causes bounding pulses and widened pulse pressure.

    Treatment of PDA includes nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) (eg, indomethacin, ibuprofen), which inhibit cyclooxygenase. Because cyclooxygenase normally synthesizes prostaglandins from arachidonic acid, NSAIDs inhibit PGE2 synthesis, thereby inducing ductal smooth muscle contraction and promoting PDA closure.

    (Choice A) Phospholipase A2 synthesizes arachidonic acid from phospholipids; therefore, phospholipase A2 activation would increase arachidonic acid metabolite production, including PGE2, and could encourage patency of the ductus arteriosus.

    (Choices B and C) Activation of prostacyclin (prostaglandin I2) and PGE2 receptors promotes vasodilation and patency, not closure, of the ductus arteriosus. This mechanism is used in treating ductal-dependent congenital heart defects (eg, transposition of the great arteries) in which maintaining ductal patency is required for adequate systemic oxygenation.

    (Choices E and F) Lipoxygenase converts arachidonic acid to leukotrienes, which are potent bronchoconstrictors. Similarly, the primary effect of prostaglandin D2 (PGD2), which is released by mast cells, is bronchoconstriction. Inhibitors of either lipoxygenase (eg, zileuton) or PGD2 may be used as bronchodilators in asthma treatment, but they are not useful in facilitating PDA closure.

    Educational objective:

    A patent ductus arteriosus (PDA) classically presents with a continuous murmur, widened pulse pressure, and bounding pulses. Because physiologic closure of the ductus arteriosus is normally initiated by loss of placental prostaglandin E2 (PGE2), treatment of a symptomatic PDA includes cyclooxygenase inhibitors (eg, indomethacin), which decrease PGE2 production.
