A newborn girl is evaluated in the nursery after an uncomplicated spontaneous vaginal delivery to a 29-year-old primigravida. The mother declined prenatal testing and ultrasound examination while pregnant. She took prenatal vitamins throughout the pregnancy, which was otherwise uneventful. Examination of the neonate shows a posterior neck mass and bilateral nonpitting edema of the hands and feet. She has low-set ears and a high-arched palate. Femoral pulses are diminished. The remainder of the examination is normal. Ultrasonography of the neck reveals a mass composed of cystic spaces separated by connective tissue. Which of the following is the most likely underlying mechanism responsible for this patient's condition?

A. Loss of function mutation on chromosome X
B. Loss of paternal copy of chromosome X
C. Meiotic nondisjunction of chromosome 18
D. Partial deletion of the short arm of chromosome 5
E. Robertsonian translocation involving chromosome 21

    • Explanation

    This patient's posterior neck mass is most likely a multiloculated cystic hygroma, which develops when obstruction of the lymphatic system causes lymph to accumulate in the jugular lymphatic sacs of the neck. Impaired flow can also cause extravasation of fluid from lymphatic vessels into the interstitial space of the hands and feet, resulting in congenital lymphedema, as seen in this patient with nonpitting extremity edema.

    In a newborn girl with cystic hygroma and/or congenital lymphedema, chromosomal aneuploidy should be suspected. In this case, the patient's diminished femoral pulses (which raise clinical suspicion for aortic coarctation) and characteristic dysmorphic features (eg, high-arched palate, low-set ears) strongly suggest Turner syndrome (TS). Other manifestations of TS include a webbed neck with low hairline and a broad chest with widely spaced nipples.

    TS is caused by complete or partial loss of an X chromosome (most commonly the paternal copy). Although genotypically normal females (46,XX) undergo X-inactivation (random silencing of one X chromosome in a given cell), some genes on the silenced X chromosome normally escape inactivation; in males, these genes also have homologous counterparts on the Y chromosome. Therefore, females with only one X chromosome are missing 1 of the normal 2 copies, causing a decreased gene dosage effect that impacts genes such as SHOX (short-stature homeobox gene) and those responsible for lymphatic development.

    (Choice A) A trinucleotide repeat expansion causing loss of function of the FMR1 gene on chromosome X is responsible for fragile X syndrome. Neonates may have dysmorphic features (eg, prominent ears), but diminished femoral pulses and cystic neck mass are not associated findings.

    (Choices C and E) Cystic hygromas and lymphedema can occur with trisomies such as Edwards syndrome (typically caused by meiotic nondisjunction of chromosome 18) and Down syndrome (usually caused by nondisjunction or robertsonian translocation of chromosome 21). However, patients with Edwards syndrome typically have hypertonia, clenched fists, and rocker-bottom feet, and patients with Down syndrome characteristically have upslanting palpebral fissures, epicanthal folds, and a single palmar crease. Down syndrome is also more likely to cause a murmur due to an atrioventricular canal defect rather than diminished pulses.

    (Choice D) Cri-du-chat syndrome is due to a partial deletion of the short arm of chromosome 5. Patients typically have a round face, cat-like cry, and microcephaly (not cystic hygroma or lymphedema).

    Educational objective:

    Turner syndrome (loss of an X chromosome) should be suspected in a newborn girl with cystic hygroma or lymphedema. Dysmorphic features (eg, high-arched palate, low-set ears) and aortic anomalies (eg, coarctation) are other characteristic findings.
