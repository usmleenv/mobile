A 12-year-old boy is brought to the office by his adoptive parents for a routine physical examination. The boy has mild intellectual disability. The parents say he is adjusting well to a new school that has more resources for children with learning disabilities. The patient was adopted as an infant, and the birth family's history is unknown. His vaccinations are up to date, and he has no allergies. Review of the patient's medical records reveals that prior cytogenetic studies showed a small gap near the tip of the long arm of the X chromosome when cells were cultured under folate-deficient conditions. Which of the following findings are likely to be present on this patient's physical examination?

A. Arachnodactyly, scoliosis, lens dislocation
B. Large ears, long face, macroorchidism
C. Short neck, low-set ears, epicanthal folds
D. Smooth philtrum, thin vermilion border
E. Tall stature, gynecomastia, small testes

    This patient with intellectual disability has cytogenetic study results consistent with fragile X syndrome (FXS). FXS is an X-linked disorder caused by unstable expansion of trinucleotide repeats (CGG) in the fragile X messenger ribonucleoprotein 1 (FMR1) gene on the X chromosome. When the cells of affected individuals are cultured in a folate-deficient medium, the area of increased repeats does not stain and appears "broken" with a small gap near the tip of the long arm of the X chromosome (hence the name fragile X).

    One of the earliest manifestations of FXS is typically developmental delay, including delayed achievement of motor and language milestones. A variable degree of intellectual disability is expected, and many patients also have neurobehavioral features of attention deficit hyperactivity disorder and/or autism spectrum disorder.

    Classic physical features of FXS tend to be subtle during infancy and become most noticeable after puberty, including a long, narrow face and prominent forehead, jaw, chin, and ears. Macroorchidism (testicular enlargement) with normal testicular function is also typical.

    (Choice A) Classic manifestations of Marfan syndrome are usually cardiac (eg, aortic root dilation, mitral valve prolapse) and musculoskeletal (eg, tall stature, arachnodactyly, scoliosis). Lens dislocation (ectopia lentis) is also common. Unlike this patient, a person with Marfan syndrome would likely have normal cognition and chromosomes.

    (Choice C) Down syndrome is a common cause of intellectual disability, and classic physical findings include short neck, low-set ears, and epicanthal folds. Cytogenetic studies usually show a karyotype with 47 chromosomes (3 copies of chromosome 21).

    (Choice D) Fetal alcohol syndrome is characterized by facial dysmorphisms (eg, short palpebral fissures, smooth philtrum, thin vermilion border) and neurobehavioral issues (eg, impairment in adaptive and executive functioning). Cytogenetic studies would be normal.

    (Choice E) Klinefelter syndrome presents with tall stature as well as gynecomastia and small, firm testes with decreased testosterone secretion. In contrast to this case, cytogenetic studies would reveal a 47,XXY karyotype.

    Educational objective:

    Fragile X syndrome is a common cause of intellectual disability. It results from unstable expansion of trinucleotide repeats (CGG) in the fragile X messenger ribonucleoprotein 1 (FMR1) gene. Classic physical findings include dysmorphic facial features (eg, long face, prominent jaw, protruding ears) and macroorchidism.
