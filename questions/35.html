A 2-day-old boy is being evaluated in the nursery for irritability. He was born to a 22-year-old primigravida who received limited prenatal care. Delivery was uncomplicated, and Apgar scores were 7 and 8 at 1 and 5 minutes, respectively. Temperature is 36.7 C (98 F), blood pressure is 70/30 mm Hg, pulse is 148/min, and respirations are 68/min. On physical examination, the patient is cyanotic and irritable. Cardiac auscultation reveals no murmurs. Serum lactate level is elevated. Echocardiogram shows the origin of the aorta lying anterior and to the right of the pulmonary artery. Failure of which of the following processes during embryonic development is most likely responsible for this patient's findings on imaging?

A. Fusion
B. Obliteration
C. Proliferation
D. Septation
E. Spiraling

    This newborn's echocardiogram is indicative of transposition of the great arteries (TGA), a cyanotic congenital heart defect characterized by abnormal origination of the aorta/pulmonary artery. In early gestation, there is a single outflow tract from the rudimentary heart that is subsequently divided into the aorta and pulmonary artery by the aorticopulmonary septum. This septum normally spirals during outflow tract division to create an anteriorly located pulmonary artery arising from the right ventricle and a posteriorly located aorta arising from the left ventricle.

    TGA occurs due to failed spiraling of the aorticopulmonary septum, resulting in an anterior aorta that originates from the right ventricle and a posterior pulmonary artery that originates from the left ventricle. Parallel pulmonary and systemic circuits are created, and deoxygenated blood is delivered from the right ventricle to the systemic circulation via the aorta.

    Neonates with TGA often have cyanosis, tachypnea, and tachycardia immediately after birth; however, some may have normal examination findings initially due to shunting of oxygenated blood to the systemic circulation via the ductus arteriosus, with cyanosis developing after 24-48 hours as the ductus begins to close. Other mixing lesions, such as a large ventricular septal defect, can also mask the development of clinically-apparent cyanosis. In this patient, elevated lactate is caused by a high degree of anaerobic metabolism due to poor oxygen delivery to the tissues.

    (Choices A and C) Failed fusion of the septum primum with the endocardial cushions leads to a primum-type atrial septal defect (ASD), whereas failed proliferation of the septum secundum leads to a secundum-type ASD. An ASD would not cause neonatal cyanosis.

    (Choice B) Failed obliteration of the ductus arteriosus results in a patent ductus arteriosus (PDA), which by itself would not explain total body neonatal cyanosis. PDA in a patient with TGA tends to delay the development of cyanosis.

    (Choice D) Failed conotruncal septation results in persistent truncus arteriosus. Although affected patients typically develop neonatal cyanosis, an echocardiogram would reveal a single arterial trunk. This patient's distinct aorta and pulmonary artery indicate conotruncal septation has occurred.

    Educational objective:

    Transposition of the great arteries is a cyanotic congenital heart condition resulting from failed spiraling of the aorticopulmonary septum during embryonic development. The aorta aberrantly originates from the right ventricle, lies anterior to the pulmonary artery, and delivers deoxygenated blood to the systemic circulation.
