    As with many other medications, phenytoin is metabolized by hepatic P450 oxidase. Plasma drug level, efficacy, and the severity of side effects are directly influenced by the rate of metabolism of phenytoin. The following features of phenytoin metabolism are important:

    • Hepatic hydroxylation of phenytoin is dose-dependent. When low doses of phenytoin are administered, a large number of free enzyme molecules are available to rapidly bind and eliminate the drug. Higher doses of phenytoin cause the enzymes to be fully saturated; increasing the dose above the saturation limit prolongs drug metabolism (switches from first- to zero-order kinetics) and leads to a rapid rise in plasma levels.
    • Phenytoin is an inducer of P450 oxidase. P450 inducers can increase the rate of elimination of medications that are metabolized by the liver, such as oral contraceptives, thereby reducing efficacy.
    • Because phenytoin itself is metabolized by P450 enzymes, its level is affected when it is co-administered with other medications that induce or inhibit the P450 system. The co-administration of phenytoin with P450 inducers (eg, rifampin, carbamazepine) decreases concentration of phenytoin in plasma and diminishes its effectiveness.

    (Choice A) Amoxicillin does not affect the hepatic metabolism of phenytoin.

    (Choices C, D and E) Fluconazole, cimetidine, and omeprazole inhibit P450 enzymes and increase the concentration of phenytoin in the serum.

    (Choice F) Oral contraceptives are metabolized by hepatic P450 oxidase, but neither induce nor inhibit the activity of P450 oxidase.

    Educational objective:

    Phenytoin metabolism depends on the function of hepatic P450 oxidases and is dose-dependent. Drugs that induce hepatic microsomal enzymes (phenobarbital, carbamazepine, and rifampin) enhance phenytoin metabolism and decrease its serum concentration.
