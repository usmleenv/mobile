An 8-year-old boy is brought to the office by his mother for a routine checkup. He was diagnosed with absence seizures a year ago and was started on appropriate treatment. The mother says that the patient is doing well and that he has had no seizures since starting medical therapy. School performance has improved significantly, and he no longer gets in trouble for "staring" in class. The patient has a well-balanced diet and is at the 50th percentile for height and weight. Which of the following is the most likely mechanism of action of the medication used in this patient?

A. Binds to GABA receptors and increases the chloride current
B. Binds to synaptic vesicles to modulate neurotransmitter release
C. Decreases the calcium current in thalamic neurons
D. Decreases the sodium current in cortical neurons
E. Increases brain beta-hydroxybutyrate acid levels

    Ethosuximide is the first-line treatment for absence seizures. This drug blocks T-type Ca2+ channels that trigger and sustain rhythmic burst discharges in thalamic neurons. It is not an effective treatment for other types of generalized or focal seizures.

    If ethosuximide is not tolerated due to adverse effects (eg, nausea/vomiting, drowsiness) or if it is ineffective in suppressing seizure activity, valproic acid is used as a second-line treatment for absence epilepsy. Valproic acid is a broad-spectrum antiseizure drug that blocks voltage-dependent Na+ channels and raises GABA concentration in the CNS.

    (Choice A) Benzodiazepines and barbiturates (eg, phenobarbital) bind to GABA receptors and allow increased chloride current through the ion channel. This hyperpolarizes the cell membrane, making it harder to trigger an action potential in the postsynaptic neuron.

    (Choice B) Levetiracetam binds to a synaptic vesicle protein (SV2A) to modulate the release of GABA and glutamate neurotransmitters.

    (Choice D) Phenytoin decreases the sodium current in cortical neurons by reducing the ability of Na+ channels to recover from inactivation. This increases the refractory period of the neuron, inhibiting neuronal high-frequency firing. Carbamazepine has a similar mechanism of action.

    (Choice E) Increasing the levels of brain beta-hydroxybutyrate (ketone bodies) is the goal of the ketogenic diet. This high-fat, low-carbohydrate diet may benefit some patients with refractory epilepsy.

    Educational objective:

    Ethosuximide, the first-line treatment for absence seizures, prevents neuronal burst firing by blocking thalamic T-type Ca2+ channels. Phenytoin, carbamazepine, and valproic acid inhibit high-frequency neuronal firing by blocking Na+ channels.
