Which of the following viral infection characteristics is most likely responsible for the pattern of illness seen in this patient?

B. Latent infection within neuronal cell bodies

D. Superinfection by a different virus

    • Explanation

    Patients with herpes simplex virus (HSV) usually have symptoms that flare and remit in a cyclical fashion. Initial HSV infections are marked by viral replication at the site of exposure (eg, perioral or genital skin), which leads to vesicular mucocutaneous lesions and flulike symptoms. Manifestations last a week or two and then resolve spontaneously when viral replication ceases. However, the virus also spreads to dermal nerve terminals and is transported in a retrograde fashion to the sensory ganglia, where it establishes a lifelong latent infection within the neuronal cell bodies.

    During latent infection, no viral replication occurs, so the disease is asymptomatic. However, infected cells continue to produce some HSV genes (eg, latency-associated transcript gene), and stress and other triggers can reactivate viral replication and lead to the re-emergence of symptoms (eg, vesicular lesions on the oral/genital mucous membranes, low-grade fever, malaise). Recurrent symptoms last a week or two and then resolve; they are often less severe than the initial episode.

    (Choice A) Although strains of malaria with dormant liver phases (eg, Plasmodium ovale, Plasmodium vivax) can cause relapsing symptoms every 6-8 weeks, this patient was diagnosed with a viral (not parasitic) infection and had infrequent relapses over years. Infection with hepatitis B or C is typically asymptomatic for years or decades; affected patients eventually develop progressive cirrhosis and related complications (eg, jaundice, ascites, encephalopathy).

    (Choice C) Epstein-Barr virus has a tropism for B-lymphocytes. Initial infection is associated with infectious mononucleosis (eg, sore throat, lymphadenopathy, malaise, atypical lymphocytosis); latent infection is usually asymptomatic, but there is a small risk of B-cell or Hodgkin lymphoma due to malignant transformation. Both Epstein-Barr virus and herpes simplex virus are members of the Herpesviridae family.

    (Choice D) Hepatitis D virus is a small RNA molecule that requires coinfection with hepatitis B virus to have complete viral assembly. Superinfection by hepatitis D virus in patients with hepatitis B virus can lead to acute hepatic failure.

    (Choice E) Influenza has a segmented genome, which leads to high rates of gene reassortment of surface glycoproteins (eg, hemagglutinin, neuraminidase). This causes new strains to emerge and can result in new infections in those with a history of previous influenza infection. However, reinfection is typically marked by symptom severity similar to previous episodes, rather than lessened severity over time.

    Educational objective:

    Patients infected with herpes simplex virus develop a lifelong latent infection of the sensory ganglion. Periodic viral replication can lead to symptoms that flare and resolve over time.
