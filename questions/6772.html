    • Explanation

    Glucose is freely filtered at the glomerulus and is promptly reabsorbed in the proximal tubule by a sodium-coupled active transport mechanism (ie, sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 [SGLT-2]). Normally, nearly all filtered glucose is reabsorbed. However, this reabsorption mechanism is saturable; at higher filtered glucose loads (ie, higher plasma glucose), reabsorption is inadequate to completely retain all of the filtered glucose, and the excess is excreted in the urine.

    SGLT-2 inhibitors (eg, canagliflozin) are a class of antidiabetic agents that reduce sodium-coupled glucose reabsorption in the proximal tubule. This reduces glucose reabsorption by the kidney (ie, increases urinary glucose loss) and lowers blood glucose levels. Concurrently, the glycosuria causes an osmotic diuresis that modestly lowers blood pressure.

    As blood glucose falls and the filtered glucose load decreases, glucose reabsorption is again adequate to reabsorb nearly all of the filtered glucose. At normal blood glucose levels, the glucose-lowering effect is minimal; therefore, these agents carry a low risk for drug-induced hypoglycemia.

    (Choice B) Thiazide diuretics (eg, chlorthalidone) decrease reabsorption of sodium, chloride, and water in the distal convoluted tubule. They can cause hyperglycemia by decreasing insulin secretion; however, they do not affect renal glucose reabsorption.

    (Choice C) Insulin has a variety of physiologic effects, but it primarily lowers blood glucose by increasing hepatic glycogen synthesis and stimulating glucose uptake in peripheral tissues. It does not alter renal glucose reabsorption.

    (Choices D, E, and F) Glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) agonists (eg, liraglutide) lower blood glucose by increasing glucose-dependent insulin secretion and decreasing glucagon secretion. Metformin lowers glucose levels by decreasing hepatic glucose production and increasing peripheral glucose uptake. Dipeptidyl peptidase-4 inhibitors (eg, sitagliptin) decrease degradation of endogenous GLP-1, leading to similar (though less potent) effects. These drugs do not affect renal glucose reabsorption.

    Educational objective:

    Glucose is reabsorbed from the proximal tubule by sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 (SGLT-2). Normally, nearly all filtered glucose is reabsorbed. SGLT-2 inhibitors lower the threshold at which filtered glucose can be fully reabsorbed; this increases urinary glucose losses and lowers blood glucose levels.
