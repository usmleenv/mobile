The patient lives in a nursing home, and his caregivers report that he has had poor oral intake over the last week.     SVR = systemic vascular resistance.

    Adrenergic agonists are often used to increase blood pressure in patients with shock. Phenylephrine is a selective alpha-1 agonist with no effect on alpha-2 receptors or beta receptors; it causes peripheral vasoconstriction, resulting in increased systemic vascular resistance (SVR) and increased blood pressure, but it has no direct effect on the heart. Drugs that increase blood pressure by increasing SVR are called pressors and are most useful in treating septic shock.

    Phenylephrine has indirect effects on the heart mediated via the baroreceptor reflex. Increased blood pressure stimulates firing of aortic and carotid baroreceptors. In response, the CNS increases parasympathetic output and decreases sympathetic output, which causes decreased heart rate and myocardial contractility. These reflexive changes slightly attenuate the initial increase in blood pressure and lead to overall reduced cardiac output.

    (Choices A and D) Dobutamine is primarily a beta-1 agonist with partial activity on beta-2 receptors and minimal effect on alpha receptors. It increases heart rate and myocardial contractility (via beta-1 stimulation) and causes mild vasodilation (via beta-2 stimulation) to decrease SVR. Milrinone is not an adrenergic agonist, but it inhibits phosphodiesterase 3 to increase myocardial contractility and cause peripheral vasodilation (decreases SVR). The combination of increased myocardial contractility (ie, inotropic effect) and reduced SVR induced by these drugs is most useful in treating cardiogenic shock.

    (Choice B) Dopamine stimulates dopamine-1 (located primarily in the renal arterioles), beta-1, and alpha-1 receptors, with the relative effects being dose dependent. High-dose dopamine primarily stimulates alpha-1 receptors to increase SVR and blood pressure; ongoing beta-1 receptor stimulation offsets the reflex‚Äìmediated decrease in heart rate, leaving the heart rate relatively unchanged.

    (Choice C) Epinephrine stimulates beta-1, beta-2, and alpha-1 receptors with dose-dependent effects. Low-dose epinephrine primarily stimulates beta-1 receptors, resulting in increased cardiac output due to increased heart rate and contractility. At low doses, the vasodilative beta-2‚Äìmediated effects are greater than the vasoconstrictive alpha-1‚Äìmediated effects, leading to a slight reduction in SVR.
