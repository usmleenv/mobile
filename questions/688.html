A 62-year-old man is brought to the emergency department by ambulance following a motor vehicle collision. The patient is obtunded but responds to painful stimuli. Blood pressure is 160/90 mm Hg, pulse is 50/min, and respirations are 10/min. Brain imaging reveals diffuse axonal injury and cerebral edema but no intracranial hematoma. Intravenous mannitol is administered to treat elevated intracranial pressure. Thirty minutes later, the patient has worsening tachypnea and hypoxemia. Chest x-ray shows new bilateral interstitial infiltrates. Which of the following is the most likely cause of this patient's respiratory distress?

A. Direct medication-induced pulmonary toxicity
B. Excessive antidiuretic hormone release
C. IgE-mediated allergic drug reaction
D. Rapid increase in intravascular volume
E. Sudden decrease in intracranial pressure

    • Explanation

    Traumatic brain injury can cause cerebral edema and increased intracranial pressure (ICP), which predisposes to cerebral hypoperfusion and herniation as the brain parenchyma expands within a fixed space. The increased ICP can be temporarily reduced with intravenous mannitol, an osmotically active agent that quickly raises plasma osmolality and therefore plasma oncotic pressure. This draws water from the interstitial space into the vascular compartment, reducing ICP but at the expense of a rapid increase in intravascular volume.

    Most patients can tolerate the rapid increase in intravascular volume, but those with a history of heart failure and elderly patients with a low reserve of left ventricular function are at risk for complications that include pulmonary edema. Pulmonary edema can occur due to an abrupt increase in pulmonary capillary hydrostatic pressure due to the heart's inability to maintain forward flow. Clinical manifestations include respiratory distress (eg, tachypnea) and hypoxemia; chest x-ray typically shows bilateral infiltrates in the interstitium and/or alveoli.

    (Choice A) Although direct medication-induced pulmonary toxicity can cause pulmonary edema, this is not typically seen with mannitol. Direct pulmonary toxicity is more common with nitrofurantoin, amiodarone, methotrexate, and some chemotherapeutic agents (eg, bleomycin, busulfan).

    (Choice B) Excessive antidiuretic hormone (ADH) release from the pituitary gland‚Äîwhich can occur in the setting of traumatic brain injury‚Äîleads to free water retention and hyponatremia. Pulmonary edema does not occur as patients remain euvolemic.

    (Choice C) IgE-mediated allergic drug reactions (ie, anaphylaxis) can lead to respiratory distress (eg, tachypnea, hypoxia) due to upper airway closure, bronchospasm, and pulmonary edema. However, hypersensitivity to mannitol would be unusual; respiratory distress following mannitol administration is far more likely to be due to the subsequent increase in intravascular volume.

    (Choice E) Although this patient has likely experienced a sudden decrease in ICP due to the intended effect of intravenous mannitol, this would not directly explain the respiratory distress.

    Educational objective:

    Intracranial pressure can be temporarily reduced with intravenous mannitol, an osmotically active agent that increases plasma oncotic pressure, causing water from the interstitial space to be drawn into the vascular compartment. In some patients (eg, history of heart failure, low left ventricular reserve function), rapidly increased intravascular volume can cause pulmonary edema by abruptly increasing pulmonary capillary hydrostatic pressure.
