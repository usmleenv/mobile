A 64-year-old man comes to the office due to slowing of his movements and gait instability that has contributed to several recent falls. The patient was diagnosed with Parkinson disease a year ago. On physical examination, there is a resting tremor involving the right hand and rigidity during passive flexion and extension of the right upper limb. The patient is started on therapy with pramipexole. Which of the following best describes the mechanism of action of this medication?

A. Decreases central dopamine degradation
B. Directly stimulates dopamine receptors
C. Enhances the effect of endogenous dopamine
D. Increases dopamine precursor availability in the brain
E. Inhibits central muscarinic receptors

    • Explanation

    This patient has Parkinson disease (PD), which is caused by degeneration of the dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra, leading to a progressive deficiency of dopamine available to inhibit the basal ganglia. This results in decreased stimulation of the motor cortex by the thalamus, leading to the classic triad of bradykinesia, rigidity, and resting tremor.

    Several categories of medications used for PD aim to treat dopamine insufficiency by different mechanisms:

    • Dopamine precursors (eg, levodopa) replace dopamine depletion
    • Enzyme inhibitors (eg, selegiline) inhibit central dopamine degradation
    • Dopamine agonists (eg, pramipexole) directly activate dopamine receptors

    Dopamine agonists (eg, pramipexole) have a chemical structure similar to that of the neurotransmitter dopamine and directly stimulate dopamine receptors. Unlike levodopa, they do not have to be metabolized to be active. In addition to having similar adverse effects as levodopa (eg, nausea, orthostasis), dopamine agonists' direct stimulation of dopamine receptors can cause impulse control disorders (eg, pathologic gambling, compulsive sexuality), likely because dopamine is involved in the rewards pathway.

    (Choice A) Selegiline inhibits monoamine oxidase-B in the brain, which results in decreased central dopamine degradation, leaving more available to stimulate the basal ganglia.

    (Choice C) Amantadine enhances the effect of endogenous dopamine by increasing dopamine release and inhibiting its reuptake. It is also thought to cause anticholinergic effects (reduces tremor), as well as antagonism of N-methyl-D-aspartate receptors (reduces glutamine-induced neurotoxicity).

    (Choice D) Both catechol-O-methyltransferase inhibitors (eg, entacapone, tolcapone) and dopa decarboxylase inhibitors (eg, carbidopa) decrease the breakdown of levodopa in peripheral tissues and increase the amount of levodopa available to cross the blood-brain barrier.

    (Choice E) The dopamine insufficiency of PD upsets the normal balance between dopamine and acetylcholine in the basal ganglia, resulting in cholinergic hypersensitivity. Anticholinergic medications (eg, trihexyphenidyl) counteract this effect by inhibiting central muscarinic receptors. These drugs are typically limited to younger patients with tremor-dominant PD due to adverse effects in the elderly and lack of effect on bradykinesia/rigidity.

    Educational objective:

    Dopamine agonists have a chemical structure similar to that of the neurotransmitter dopamine and directly stimulate dopamine receptors. They can be used instead of, or in addition to, levodopa therapy to treat the motor symptoms of Parkinson disease (eg, bradykinesia, rigidity, tremor).
