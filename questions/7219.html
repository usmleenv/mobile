    • Explanation

    Vancomycin inhibits cell wall synthesis of gram-positive bacteria by binding to the D-Alanine-D-Alanine terminus of nascent peptidoglycan pentapeptides, thereby inhibiting peptidoglycan elongation and cross-linking. Although vancomycin is generally well tolerated, a minority of patients develop vancomycin infusion reaction (VIR), a non-allergic drug reaction that occurs when vancomycin is infused too rapidly.

    VIR arises due to the direct activation of mast cells by vancomycin, which results in the release of vasoactive mediators (eg, histamine). Manifestations typically arise within seconds/minutes of vancomycin infusion and include flushing, erythema, and pruritus, primarily in the neck and upper torso. Most cases resolve spontaneously with the cessation of vancomycin and the administration of diphenhydramine. Because VIR is not a true allergic reaction (not IgE-mediated), vancomycin can be safely resumed at a slower rate once the symptoms have resolved.

    (Choice A) Gentamicin, an aminoglycoside antibiotic, is associated with ototoxicity and nephrotoxicity.

    (Choices B and C) Beta-lactam antibiotics (eg, imipenem, penicillin) generally cause IgE-mediated hypersensitivity reactions. In allergic reactions, histamine release is not linked to infusion rate, and patients usually have a history of previous exposure.

    (Choice D) Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole can occasionally cause megaloblastic anemia, thrombocytopenia, leukopenia, and/or toxic epidermal necrolysis.

    Educational objective:

    Rapid infusion of vancomycin can lead to the direct activation of mast cells, which results in a massive release of histamine. This causes vancomycin infusion reaction, a nonallergic reaction marked by flushing, erythema, and pruritus within seconds or minutes of vancomycin infusion. Because this reaction is not IgE-mediated, vancomycin can be safely resumed at a slower rate once symptoms have resolved.
