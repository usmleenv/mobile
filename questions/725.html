A 4-year-old boy is brought to the office due to skin lesions that appeared on his chin 2 days ago. Temperature is 36.1 C (97 F). The patient appears well. Mildly tender perioral papules and pustules are noted on examination, some of which have broken down and are covered with amber-colored crusting. A specimen is collected from the wound. Gram stain shows gram-positive cocci in chains. Cultures are pending. Which of the following symptoms would be most likely to develop as a potential complication of this patient's infection?

A. Ascending weakness
B. Facial puffiness and dark urine
C. Fatigue and heart murmur
D. Joint pain and eye redness
E. Unilateral facial drooping

    This child has impetigo, a superficial bacterial infection that most commonly develops on the face or extremities in children. Patients usually have mildly itchy or painful erythematous papules and pustules that later erupt, forming a honey-colored crusting. The most common pathogen is Staphylococcus aureus followed by group A Streptococcus (GAS), specifically, Streptococcus pyogenes. In this case, microscopy of the patient's exudate shows gram-positive cocci in chains, indicative of streptococcal infection.

    Localized impetigo is treated with topical antibiotics and most patients have no long-term sequelae. However, a small number of patients with impetigo due to GAS develop poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis (PSGN) several weeks after the initial infection. Kidney damage is caused by immune complex deposition and subsequent complement activation, precipitating an inflammatory response that damages the glomerular basement membrane.

    Presentations of PSGN range from asymptomatic, microscopic hematuria to overt nephritic syndrome; pyuria and nephritic-range (<3.5 g/day) proteinuria are common. In addition to tea- or cola-colored urine, symptomatic patients can develop periorbital/facial swelling, generalized edema, and hypertension. Laboratory evaluation typically reveals elevated creatinine and decreased C3 levels.

    (Choice A) Ascending weakness is a classic feature of Guillain-Barr√© syndrome, which may be preceded by an upper respiratory or gastrointestinal (eg, Campylobacter jejuni) tract infection. GAS infection is not associated with this condition.

    (Choice C) Fatigue and a new heart murmur due to carditis can be manifestations of acute rheumatic fever (ARF), a potential poststreptococcal sequela classically associated with GAS pharyngitis. However, ARF is not associated with impetigo in most regions of the world.

    (Choice D) Eye redness (due to conjunctivitis or anterior uveitis) and joint pain may be seen in reactive arthritis, which typically occurs following a diarrheal (eg, Salmonella) or genitourinary tract (eg, Chlamydia) infection, not impetigo.

    (Choice E) Unilateral facial drooping due to a peripheral facial nerve palsy is most commonly idiopathic (ie, Bell palsy) or associated with neuroborreliosis or herpes simplex virus infection.

    Educational objective:

    Poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis is a potential complication of group A streptococcal infection (eg, impetigo, pharyngitis). Symptoms include proteinuria (often associated with periorbital or generalized edema) and hematuria. In most regions of the world, acute rheumatic fever is a postinfectious sequela of streptococcal pharyngitis only, not impetigo.
