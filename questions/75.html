A 35-year-old man comes to the office due to fatigue, dyspnea on exertion, and lower extremity edema that have worsened over the past several weeks. The patient has been generally healthy, but 2 months ago he experienced an episode of fever, runny nose, and myalgia that resolved after a few days. The patient takes no medications and has no significant family history. He occasionally drinks alcohol and does not use tobacco or recreational drugs. Blood pressure is 112/74 mm Hg and pulse is 98/min and regular. Physical examination reveals jugular venous distension, bibasilar lung crackles, and 2+ lower extremity pitting edema. Which of the following echocardiographic findings is most likely to be seen in this patient?

A. Concentric left ventricle thickening with abnormal diastolic relaxation
B. Diffusely dilated ventricles with decreased systolic function
C. Increased systolic pressure gradient across the aortic valve
D. Left ventricular outflow obstruction by systolic anterior motion of the mitral valve
E. Regional abnormality of ventricular wall motion during systole

    This patient's presentation with dyspnea, lower extremity edema, jugular venous distension, and lung crackles is consistent with decompensated heart failure. In a young patient who develops heart failure following a viral prodrome (eg, fever, sinus congestion, myalgia), viral myocarditis progressing to dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) should be suspected. A number of viruses have been implicated as causes of DCM, including coxsackievirus, adenovirus, and influenza virus. Some patients may have an inadequate immune response that allows the virus to persist inside cardiomyocytes, resulting in a direct cytotoxic effect or a destructive autoimmune reaction.

    DCM involves depletion of cardiomyocytes with subsequent fibrosis causing weakened myocardial contractility (systolic dysfunction) and volume overload; prominent ventricular dilation (eccentric hypertrophy) occurs as surviving myocytes respond to increasing wall stress.

    (Choice A) Concentric left ventricular (LV) hypertrophy with thickened walls and abnormal diastolic relaxation is characteristic of hypertensive heart disease due to long-standing uncontrolled hypertension. Initially, the LV cavity is reduced in size and contractile function is normal, but over time contractile dysfunction and LV cavity enlargement can develop.

    (Choice C) Increased systolic pressure gradient across the aortic valve is present in aortic stenosis, and concentric LV hypertrophy can occur due to LV pressure overload. Aortic stenosis can develop in relatively young patients due to calcification of a bicuspid aortic valve, but it is unlikely to cause heart failure over a matter of weeks in a patient age <40.

    (Choice D) In patients with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, systolic anterior motion of the mitral valve may worsen LV outflow tract obstruction. Patients commonly present with exertional dyspnea or syncope (or sometimes sudden cardiac death); presentation with decompensated heart failure is less common.

    (Choice E) A regional ventricular systolic wall motion abnormality (ie, regional hypokinesis) is suggestive of ischemic heart disease (eg, myocardial infarction). In contrast, myocarditis and DCM typically demonstrate a diffuse reduction in contractile function (ie, diffuse hypokinesis). Ischemic heart disease is unlikely in this young patient who has no significant risk factors for cardiovascular disease.

    Educational objective:

    Dilated cardiomyopathy results from direct damage to cardiomyocytes and consequent myocardial contractile dysfunction (systolic dysfunction), volume overload, and ventricular dilation. Viral myocarditis is a potential cause of dilated cardiomyopathy and should be suspected in young patients who develop heart failure following a viral prodrome.
