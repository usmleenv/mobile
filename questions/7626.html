A 2-year-old boy is brought to the office due to poor weight gain and frequent urination. His mother says he has not gained any weight since his well child visit 6 months ago, and in the last several months he has been urinating more than 10 times a day. His weight percentile has decreased from 60th percentile to 10th percentile, consistent with failure to thrive. Physical examination reveals frontal bossing. He is found to have glucosuria on urinalysis, although his serum glucose is within normal limits. Additional serum laboratory studies reveal hypophosphatemia, hypokalemia, and a normal anion gap metabolic acidosis. Which of the following structure/function combinations is most likely defective in this patient?

    • Explanation

    This patient's glycosuria with normal serum glucose in combination with normal anion gap metabolic acidosis, hypokalemia, and hypophosphatemia is highly suggestive of impaired proximal tubular reabsorption. The proximal convoluted tubule is responsible for reabsorption of bicarbonate in addition to amino acids, phosphate, glucose, water, and potassium. Impaired bicarbonate reabsorption can occur as an isolated defect (renal tubular acidosis [RTA] type 2) or as part of a more generalized proximal tubular dysfunction (Fanconi syndrome):

    • Loss of bicarbonate causes persistent acidosis.
    • Hypophosphatemia leads to rickets (eg, frontal bossing, leg bowing).
    • Glucosuria contributes to polyuria (impaired renal concentrating ability also contributes). Serum glucose levels are normal, as seen here, because they are tightly regulated by multiple mechanisms (eg, insulin, glucagon) that are independent of renal reabsorption.
    • Hypokalemia can lead to constipation and muscle weakness.
    • Aminoaciduria, contributing to growth failure.

    (Choices A and D) The proximal tubule is also responsible for the generation of ammonia (NH3). Hypoaldosteronism or aldosterone resistance (RTA type 4, most commonly seen in adults with diabetes) can disrupt this process by inhibiting production of NH3 in the proximal tubule, resulting in reduced ammonium (NH4+) excretion and metabolic acidosis. This patient's hypokalemia and hypophosphatemia are inconsistent with this diagnosis.

    (Choices B, C, and F) Hydrogen ions (H+) are primarily secreted into the urine from the distal convoluted tubule. Failure of this process (RTA type 1) leads to decreased distal acid secretion and metabolic acidosis, but phosphate and glucose would be normal.

    Educational objective:

    Defects in either the proximal or distal convoluted tubule (eg, bicarbonate loss, acid retention) can result in a normal anion gap metabolic acidosis. However, the proximal tubule is responsible for reabsorption of glucose, amino acids, and phosphate in addition to bicarbonate; therefore, generalized proximal tubular dysfunction (Fanconi syndrome) can also cause failure to thrive, glucosuria, and rickets.
