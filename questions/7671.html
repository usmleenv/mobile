    The physiologic effects of pituitary tumors are typically due to excess production of hormones and correlate with the cell of origin. Large tumors, even nonphysiologically active (nonfunctioning) adenomas, can cause endocrine abnormalities due to compression of the surrounding normal pituitary gland.

    Prolactin-secreting adenomas (prolactinomas) are the most common hormonally active pituitary tumors and can cause severe hyperprolactinemia. Abnormal prolactin secretion can also occur in nonprolactinoma tumors because, unlike secretion of other pituitary hormones, prolactin secretion is under negative regulation by dopamine from the hypothalamus; any disruption of dopaminergic pathways in the pituitary stalk can cause a moderate increase in prolactin release.

    (Choice A) Aldosterone is produced in the adrenal cortex, not the pituitary. It is regulated primarily by the renin-angiotensin system.

    (Choice B) Circulating catecholamines arise primarily from the adrenal medulla and, to a lesser extent, from spillover from sympathetic nervous system synapses. Neuroendocrine tumors (eg, pheochromocytoma) can cause excess catecholamine release.

    (Choice C) Most gonadotroph adenomas are hormonally nonfunctioning; ovarian hyperstimulation with an elevated estradiol level is uncommon. In addition, prolactinomas commonly cause central hypogonadism with low estradiol levels due to suppression of GnRH release from the hypothalamus.

    (Choice D) The effects of growth hormone (GH) are largely mediated by insulin-like growth factor-1, which is produced in the liver in response to GH. However, somatotroph adenomas (ie, GH-secreting tumors) are relatively rare and significantly less common than prolactin-secreting adenomas.

    (Choice E) Melatonin is produced by the pineal gland and regulates circadian rhythm.

    (Choice G) Vasopressin is produced in the hypothalamus and released by the posterior pituitary. In general, pituitary lesions have little effect on circulating vasopressin because, even if the posterior pituitary is compressed, synthesis continues in the hypothalamus and the hormone can be released from nerve fibers in the hypothalamo-hypophyseal tract.

    Educational objective:

    Prolactinomas, the most common hormonally active pituitary tumors, can cause severe hyperprolactinemia. Moderate hyperprolactinemia can also occur in nonprolactinoma tumors because prolactin secretion is under negative regulation by dopamine from the hypothalamus.
