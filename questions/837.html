A 34-year-old primigravida at 18 weeks gestation comes to the office for a routine prenatal examination. The patient's pregnancy has been uncomplicated. She takes a prenatal vitamin daily, and her laboratory results have been normal to date. The patient's personal and family medical histories are unremarkable. During the visit, a detailed fetal ultrasound examination reveals severe unilateral hydronephrosis; there is no ureteral dilation. Male external genitalia are also visible. If the fetal hydronephrosis is caused by obstruction, which of the following is the most likely site?

A. Spinal cord
B. Ureteropelvic junction
C. Urethra
D. Urinary meatus
E. Vesicoureteral junction

    • Explanation

    Fetal hydronephrosis is a common finding on routine ultrasound examination during the second trimester. The finding is most commonly unilateral, mild, and transient, likely related to a temporary narrowing in the ureteropelvic junction (UPJ) during normal development, as the fetal renal pelvis is highly compliant and susceptible to dilation. In this case, however, the dilation is severe, making a pathologic cause more likely.

    The most common pathologic cause of antenatal hydronephrosis is persistent UPJ obstruction due to an embryologic defect that develops as follows:

    • The fetal genitourinary tract is derived from the metanephric blastema and the ureteric bud (a dorsal outgrowth of the mesonephric duct). The metanephric blastema develops into functional renal parenchyma by 10 weeks gestation while the ureteric bud develops into the renal pelvis and ureters through sequential dilation and canalization. The UPJ is the last segment of the fetal ureter to canalize.
    • The pathogenesis of fetal UPJ obstruction likely involves canalization failure resulting in stenosis of the proximal ureter. Most cases are unilateral and cause hydronephrosis with calyceal/renal pelvic dilation and without ureteral dilation. If the condition is not diagnosed prenatally, it may present with a palpable abdominal mass (enlarged kidney) on examination.

    (Choice A) Neurogenic causes of obstructive uropathy (eg, spinal cord damage) result in bilateral hydronephrosis along with hydroureter due to dysfunctional voiding.

    (Choices C and D) Obstruction of the urethra or urinary meatus causes bilateral, not unilateral, hydronephrosis in addition to ureteral dilation. The most common cause in boys is posterior urethral valves or obstructive urogenital membranes at the junction of the bladder and urethra. Other congenital anomalies at this anatomic level that would also cause bilateral fetal hydronephrosis include urethral strictures, meatal stenosis, and bladder neck obstruction.

    (Choice E) Abnormalities at the vesicoureteral junction (eg, congenital anomaly, renal calculi) can cause obstruction but are rare and would lead to ureteral dilation (not seen here) if severe enough to cause hydronephrosis. Similarly, vesicoureteral reflux (VUR), a common cause of fetal hydronephrosis, is also associated with ureteral dilation/tortuosity.

    Educational objective:

    Inadequate canalization of the ureter at the ureteropelvic junction (the connection site between the kidney and the ureter) is the most common cause of severe, unilateral fetal hydronephrosis.
