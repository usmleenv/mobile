A 37-year-old man is found unresponsive next to a tree during a thunderstorm. He is not breathing when paramedics arrive. On examination, the pupils are fixed and dilated. Several erythematous marks in a fern-like pattern are seen on the skin of his lower extremities. Partial-thickness burns are present on both arms. Cardiopulmonary resuscitation is started; there is no return of spontaneous circulation, and the patient is pronounced dead shortly after arrival at the hospital. Which of the following is the most likely primary cause of death?

A. Carbon monoxide poisoning
B. Cardiac arrhythmia
C. Extensive deep tissue burns
D. Intracranial hemorrhage
E. Pulmonary barotrauma
F. Upper airway edema

    *Pathognomonic skin finding in lightning injury.

    This unconscious person who was found near trees during a thunderstorm has sustained a lightning injury; the presence of Lichtenberg figures (ie, erythematous cutaneous marks in a fern-like pattern) and partial-thickness burns confirms the mechanism of injury. Although lightning injuries are uncommon, they are associated with a 25% fatality rate, and more than 70% of survivors have long-lasting complications. Two-thirds of lightning-related deaths occur within the first hour following injury; the most common causes are fatal arrhythmias and central respiratory failure.

    A comprehensive and thorough physical examination should always be performed on lightning strike victims as injury patterns vary widely and assessment based solely on skin findings can significantly underestimate the extent of internal injury.

    (Choices A and F) Smoke inhalation during a fire can cause carbon monoxide poisoning and upper airway edema. However, smoke exposure is less likely in an outdoor setting with no evidence of a nearby fire, and superficial burns in a fern-like pattern (Lichtenberg figures) are pathognomonic for lightning injury.

    (Choice C) Superficial and partial-thickness burns are common in lightning injury. In contrast, deep tissue burns are relatively rare due to the short duration of electrical contact and the flashover effect, which is the tendency of lightning to travel over the skin surface and discharge to the ground. This effect is responsible for the formation of Lichtenberg figures.

    (Choice D) Intracranial hemorrhage only rarely occurs following lightning injury. Lightning strikes can damage the autonomic nervous system and cause the pupils to become fixed and dilated or asymmetric. These signs may not accurately reflect the extent of neurologic injury and should not be a reason to stop resuscitation efforts.

    (Choice E) Lightning induces rapid heating of the surrounding air, generating high-energy shock waves that can travel through the body and cause mechanical trauma. For this reason, patients should be thoroughly assessed for bone fractures. Pulmonary barotrauma can also occur but is rare.

    Educational objective:

    Although lightning injuries are uncommon, they are associated with a 25% fatality rate. Two-thirds of lightning-related deaths occur within the first hour after injury, with fatal arrhythmias and central respiratory failure as the most common causes. Patients with minor cutaneous involvement may still have major internal trauma following a lightning injury.
