    Major functions of hypothalamic nuclei
    Ventromedial Mediates satiety; destruction leads to hyperphagia
    Lateral Mediates hunger; destruction leads to anorexia
    Anterior Mediates heat dissipation; destruction leads to hyperthermia
    Posterior Mediates heat conservation; destruction leads to hypothermia
    Arcuate Secretion of dopamine (inhibits prolactin), GHRH
    Medial preoptic Secretion of GnRH, regulates sexual behavior
    Paraventricular Secretion of oxytocin, CRH, TRH & small amounts of ADH
    Supraoptic Secretion of ADH & small amounts of oxytocin
    Suprachiasmatic Circadian rhythm regulation & pineal gland function
    ADH = antidiuretic hormone; CRH = corticotropin-releasing hormone; GHRH = growth hormone-releasing hormone; GnRH = gonadotropin-releasing hormone; TRH = thyrotropin-releasing hormone.

    This patient is experiencing jet lag, a syndrome associated with long flights that cross several time zones. Jet lag is caused by dyssynchrony between the body's circadian rhythms (ie, internal 24-hour sleep/wake clock) and the local environment (eg, daylight hours, work schedule). Symptoms include insomnia, daytime drowsiness, decreased work performance, gastrointestinal issues, and generalized malaise.

    Circadian rhythms are maintained by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), which receives input from the retina and sends projections to other hypothalamic nuclei and the pineal gland. The SCN functions primarily to modulate behavior (eg, alertness) and diurnal variations in body temperature and hormone production (eg, cortisol, melatonin). Circulating melatonin levels are normally highest during the night and lowest during the day. In contrast, cortisol peaks in the morning and reaches its lowest level at night.

    In patients with jet lag, the normal variations in light exposure at the destination are able to resynchronize the body clock to coincide with the local environment over the course of several days. Eastward travel takes longer to recover from than westward travel because it is easier to lengthen the circadian rhythm than to shorten it. Treatment with specifically-timed light exposure or melatonin administration can help reduce the adjustment period.

    Educational objective:

    The suprachiasmatic nucleus regulates circadian rhythms by processing light clues in the environment to modulate alertness, body temperature, and production of hormones (eg, cortisol, melatonin). Dyssynchrony between the local environment (eg, daylight hours, work schedules) and internal circadian rhythm can cause insomnia and daytime sleepiness (ie, jet lag).
