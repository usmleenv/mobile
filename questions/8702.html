    This patient with pituitary enlargement on brain imaging and bitemporal hemianopia (ie, temporal visual field loss due to compression of the optic chiasm) most likely has a pituitary adenoma. These benign tumors arise from the anterior pituitary and are the most common cause of sellar masses in adults. Secondary hypogonadism (eg, amenorrhea) frequently occurs in patients with pituitary tumors, and can be caused by excessive prolactin secretion (which suppresses hypothalamic secretion of GnRH) or direct compression of gonadotrope cells by the expanding mass.

    All tissues are derivatives of the 3 primary germ layers (ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm) that form during gastrulation. As epiblast cells migrate downward through the primitive streak to form the mesoderm and endoderm layers, a portion of them also migrate cranially through the primitive node (the cephalic end of the primitive streak) to form a midline, mesoderm-derived cellular cord known as the notochord. The notochord induces the overlying ectoderm to differentiate into the neuroectoderm and form the neural plate, which gives rise to the neural crest and neural tube (Choices C and D); the remainder of the ectoderm becomes the surface ectoderm.

    The anterior pituitary is derived from the Rathke pouch, an evagination of the surface ectoderm that lines the fetal oral cavity (Choices A and B). In contrast, the posterior pituitary (pars nervosa and pituitary stalk) is derived from the infundibulum, an extension of neuroectoderm from the diencephalon; posterior pituitary tumors are rare.

    (Choice E) The notochord becomes the nucleus pulposus of the intervertebral discs in adults

    Educational objective:

    Pituitary adenoma is a benign tumor arising from the anterior pituitary and the most common cause of sellar masses (headache, bitemporal hemianopia, hypogonadism) in adults. The anterior pituitary forms from the surface ectoderm, one of the 3 tissues derived from the ectoderm (ie, surface ectoderm, neural tube, neural crest).
