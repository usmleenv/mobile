    Immunohistochemistry (IHC) uses tagged antibodies to identify cellular antigens of interest (eg, cytoskeletal proteins, surface molecules). Specific applications in the diagnosis and management of cancer include:

    • Identifying the tissue of origin: Detection of antigens that are specific to certain cells can help characterize the primary tumor (eg, differentiating between various primary CNS malignancies). It can also help determine the source of a metastasis if the primary site is clinically unknown, particularly for anaplastic/poorly differentiated tumors lacking architectural clues.
    • Predicting tumor behavior and guiding treatment: Identification of certain markers (eg, estrogen receptor, HER2 expression in breast carcinoma) can predict a tumor's biological behavior (eg, level of aggressiveness) and response to specific therapies (eg, trastuzumab).

    Glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP) is an intermediate filament protein normally expressed in glial cells (eg, astrocytes, ependymal cells, oligodendrocytes); tumors of glial lineage are often positive for GFAP (Choices A, B, and E). In contrast, synaptophysin is a transmembrane glycoprotein normally found in the presynaptic vesicles of neurons and neuroendocrine cells; brain tumors with neuronal or neuroendocrine differentiation are typically positive for synaptophysin.

    (Choice C) Meningothelial cells are found within the arachnoid membranes and can give rise to meningiomas; they do not express GFAP or synaptophysin.

    Educational objective:

    Glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP) is normally found in glia, and tumors of glial lineage are often positive for GFAP. Synaptophysin is normally found in neurons and neuroendocrine cells; tumors with neuronal or neuroendocrine differentiation are typically positive for synaptophysin.
