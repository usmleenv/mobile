A 43-year-old man comes to the office due to shortness of breath and fatigue. Over the last 2 weeks, his fatigue has been so profound that he has "little energy, even to get out of bed." The patient has no chills but has experienced recent weight gain and ankle swelling. He has no prior medical conditions and takes no medications. Blood pressure is 168/94 mm Hg, and pulse is 95/min and regular. The patient has bilateral lower extremity pitting edema limited to the ankles. Urinalysis reveals 2+ protein, a white blood cell count of 5-7/hpf, and a red blood cell count of 75-100/hpf. The patient undergoes a kidney biopsy; immunofluorescent microscopy findings are shown in the image below.

Which of the following would most likely be seen on light microscopy in this patient?

A. Amyloid deposition
B. Crescent formation
C. Diffuse capillary wall thickening
D. Nodular glomerulosclerosis
E. Normal glomeruli

    • Explanation

    This patient's hypertension, hematuria (with mild pyuria), and moderate proteinuria are indicative of nephritic syndrome. Immunofluorescence microscopy further demonstrates linear deposits (ie, ribbon-like) along the glomerular basement membrane (GBM), a finding characteristic of anti-GBM disease. In this condition, anti-GBM autoantibodies (usually IgG) develop against the alpha-3 chain of type IV collagen (a subunit found in high concentration in the GBM). Subsequent complement deposition (eg, C3) and leukocyte infiltration lead to rapidly progressive (crescentic) glomerulonephritis (RPGN).

    RPGN is a syndrome of severe renal injury that manifests with acute-onset hematuria and decreased glomerular filtration (weight gain, edema). It can occur due to multiple diseases (eg, granulomatosis with polyangiitis, microscopic polyangiitis). The presence of glomerular crescents‚Äîcomposed of proliferating parietal cells, lymphocytes, macrophages, and fibrin‚Äîon light microscopy is diagnostic.

    Anti-GBM antibodies also cross-react with pulmonary alveolar basement membranes, which are also enriched in the alpha-3 subchain, causing diffuse alveolar damage and hemorrhage. The combination of glomerulonephritis and pulmonary hemorrhage in patients with anti-GBM antibodies is known as Goodpasture syndrome.

    (Choice A) Renal amyloidosis causes a nephrotic syndrome (ie, heavy proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia, hyperlipidemia, edema); significant hematuria is unexpected. Immunofluorescence microscopy is nonspecific and may show diffuse mesangial staining (rather than distinct, linear immune deposits on the GBM). On Congo red staining, amyloid deposits appear red-pink under light microscopy and have an apple-green birefringence under polarized light.

    (Choice C) In membranous nephropathy, which causes a nephrotic syndrome, immunofluorescence demonstrates granular deposits of IgG and C3 along the GBM. Uniform, diffuse capillary wall thickening is seen on light microscopy.

    (Choice D) Nodular glomerulosclerosis (Kimmelstiel-Wilson lesion) and mesangial expansion are seen on light microscopy in diabetic nephropathy, which presents as a nephrotic syndrome. There are no immune deposits on immunofluorescence.

    (Choice E) Normal glomeruli are found on light microscopy in minimal change disease, a condition that primarily affects children and presents as a nephrotic syndrome. There are no immune deposits on immunofluorescence.

    Educational objective:

    Anti-glomerular basement membrane (GBM) antibodies react with collagen type IV, causing rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis with glomerular crescent formation on light microscopy. Immunofluorescence demonstrating linear (ribbon-like) deposits of IgG and C3 along the GBM is characteristic.
