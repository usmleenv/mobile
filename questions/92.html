A 42-year-old man comes to the emergency department due to fatigue and exertional dyspnea. Over the past 2 weeks, his symptoms have progressively worsened to the point that he can no longer walk across his living room without becoming short of breath. He is admitted to the hospital but dies suddenly despite aggressive treatment. An autopsy is performed and a cross-section of the heart is shown below.

Which of the following mechanisms is most likely to be primarily responsible for this patient's symptoms?

A. Decreased ventricular contraction force
B. Dynamic left ventricular outflow obstruction
C. Impaired left ventricular filling
D. Increased pulmonary vascular resistance
E. Reduced ventricular wall compliance

    The gross specimen of this patient's heart demonstrates left ventricular (LV) cavity enlargement consistent with eccentric hypertrophy, which is an initially adaptive mechanism that allows the LV to maintain cardiac output in the setting of increasing volume overload. Multiple disease processes can lead to eccentric hypertrophy, including ischemic heart disease, certain types of valvular disease (ie, aortic regurgitation, mitral regurgitation), and dilated cardiomyopathy (primary myocardial dysfunction due to viral infection, chronic alcohol abuse, inherited disease).

    Eventually, progressive eccentric enlargement of the LV becomes maladaptive, with overwhelming wall stress leading to decreased ventricular contractility. Reduced cardiac output leads to decompensated heart failure with fatigue, dyspnea, orthopnea, and peripheral edema. The structural changes to the left ventricle also increase the risk of ventricular arrhythmia (ie, ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation), a potential cause of sudden cardiac death in these patients.

    (Choice B) Dynamic LV outflow tract obstruction can be seen in hypertrophic cardiomyopathy as the degree of LV outflow tract obstruction varies with changes in LV blood volume. Asymmetric hypertrophy of the LV septal wall and a small LV cavity are expected with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy.

    (Choices C and E) Reduced ventricular wall compliance leads to impaired LV filling (diastolic dysfunction). This most commonly occurs as the initial manifestation of hypertensive heart disease when pressure overload leads to concentric LV hypertrophy with thickened LV walls and a small LV cavity. Reduced LV wall compliance is also typically present in restrictive cardiomyopathy, severe aortic stenosis, and hypertrophic cardiomyopathy. In contrast, LV compliance is increased with the cavity dilation that occurs in dilated cardiomyopathy; although some degree of diastolic dysfunction can also develop, this occurs secondary to elevated LV filling pressures that result from the contractile dysfunction.

    (Choice D) Increased pulmonary vascular resistance can cause right-sided heart failure due to pulmonary hypertension. There is initially right ventricular (RV) wall thickening that can eventually progress to contractile dysfunction and RV cavity enlargement. This patient's RV wall thickness and cavity size appear normal.

    Educational objective:

    Dilation of the left ventricular cavity commonly occurs in response to systolic dysfunction (eg, ischemic heart disease, dilated cardiomyopathy) or certain types of valvular disease (ie, aortic regurgitation, mitral regurgitation). Chronic volume overload causes progressive eccentric hypertrophy that eventually leads to reduced ventricular contractility and decompensated heart failure.
